Holy Land: A Pilgrim's Guide to Indulgences

March 2, 2026  ·  Ecclesia Dev

Rome is where the Church is buried. Jerusalem is where God walked. The pilgrim who arrives in the Holy Land is not walking through a museum or even a memory — he is walking through the geography of the Redemption itself. Every stone here is a datum in salvation history. The Via Dolorosa is not a street that resembles the road Christ carried the Cross; it is that road, or as close to it as two thousand years of conquest, earthquake, and construction can preserve. The hill of Calvary is still there, encased inside the Church of the Holy Sepulchre. The grotto where God became man is still there under the Basilica of the Nativity.

The Conditions — Always, for Everything

The Church has attached indulgences to these sites accordingly. The Franciscan Custody of the Holy Land — the Latin guardians of these shrines since Pope Clement VI formally entrusted them in 1342 — holds specific grants of indulgences attached to prayers at the major shrines. Some are confirmed in the Enchiridion Indulgentiarum. Others derive from papal grants to the Custody. When in doubt on any specific grant, the correct authority is the Custodia di Terra Santa directly.

For a plenary indulgence, you need all four:

  1. Sacramental confession — within approximately twenty days before or after the prescribed work
  2. Holy Communion — received on the same day as the indulgenced work
  3. Prayer for the Pope's intentions — an Our Father and Hail Mary suffice
  4. Complete detachment from all sin, including venial — the condition most pilgrims fail without realizing it

Without the fourth condition, a plenary indulgence becomes partial. Many pilgrims confess before departing, receive at the Church of the Holy Sepulchre, pray the right prayers, and gain only a partial indulgence because they are still attached to some habitual sin. Examine your conscience before you leave home, not on the flight over.

Jerusalem

The Church of the Holy Sepulchre

The most sacred building in Christendom, and the most complicated. Under one roof: the hill of Golgotha where Christ was crucified, the Stone of Anointing where His body was prepared for burial, and the Edicule — the shrine encasing the actual Tomb. The church was first built by Constantine in the fourth century; what stands today is largely Crusader construction with centuries of additions, renovations, and damage.

The Status Quo: Before you enter, understand what you are walking into. The Status Quo is an arrangement codified by Ottoman decree in 1852 that freezes the custodial rights of the major Christian communities at their state as of that moment. The church is shared primarily among three communities: the Greek Orthodox (largest portion, including the main nave called the Katholikon and primary rights at the Edicule), the Latin Catholics under Franciscan Custody (Chapel of the Nailing to the Cross and Chapel of the Apparition, among others), and the Armenian Apostolic Church. The Copts, Ethiopians, and Syriac Orthodox hold smaller but legally fixed rights. The key on the front door of the church is held by a Muslim family — the Joudeh family for custody, the Nuseibeh family for opening — a Saladin-era arrangement that has kept the peace between Christian communities for eight centuries.

This matters for the pilgrim because you cannot simply set up a private Mass anywhere you wish inside the church. The Franciscan friars celebrate Mass daily in the Latin chapels at fixed times. Find those times; attend them.

What to do:

  • Calvary/Golgotha: Climb the stairs immediately to the right upon entering. The Latin chapel is on the right; the Greek Orthodox chapel of the actual Crucifixion is on the left. There is a hole through glass beneath the Greek altar where you can reach down and touch the rock of Calvary itself. Do this. Kneel. Pray slowly.
  • The Stone of Anointing: The large flat stone near the entrance, covered in red stone and surrounded by hanging lamps. It marks the traditional site where Christ's body was anointed before burial. Pilgrims kneel and press their foreheads to it.
  • The Edicule/Tomb: Expect a queue. It can run two hours in high season. You will have approximately ninety seconds inside. Do not waste them. Kneel at the stone slab where the body of Our Lord lay. Pray the Creed. This is the center of the universe.

Indulgences: A plenary indulgence is attached to devout visitation of the major stations inside the Church of the Holy Sepulchre (the Tomb, Calvary, the Stone of Anointing) with the prescribed prayers. The Franciscan Custody holds specific grants for prayers at each of these points — verify current conditions with the Franciscan Custody before traveling, as grant language and prescribed prayers should be confirmed against the current Enchiridion and the Custody's own published guidance.

Via Dolorosa — The 14 Stations of the Cross

The Way of Sorrow runs from the Antonia Fortress site (Stations I–II, near the Lion's Gate / St. Stephen's Gate) through the Muslim Quarter and Christian Quarter of the Old City, terminating at the Church of the Holy Sepulchre (Stations X–XIV). The route passes through the active market streets of the souk, which does not diminish it — Christ carried His Cross through a crowded, indifferent city too.

The indulgence: A plenary indulgence is attached to walking the Via Dolorosa devoutly, meditating on the Passion at each station, with the standard conditions. This is among the most firmly established Holy Land indulgences, granted by the Holy See to the Franciscan Custody and applied to pilgrims who make the full devotional walk.

The Franciscans lead an official Via Dolorosa procession every Friday afternoon — typically departing from Station I at around 3:00pm. Join it. The presence of the friars, the prayers chanted in common, and the crowds of pilgrims from around the world transform what might otherwise feel like a walking tour into something recognizably liturgical.

The stations are marked by numbered plaques on the walls. Some are inside churches (Stations I–II inside the Franciscan Chapel of the Condemnation and adjacent school compound; Stations IV–V along the street; Station VI at the Church of the Holy Face/St. Veronica's). The final five stations (X–XIV) are inside the Church of the Holy Sepulchre itself — the stripping of garments, nailing to the cross, death, descent from the cross, and burial.

Walk it slowly. The old city is not large.

The Garden of Gethsemane / Basilica of the Agony

At the foot of the Mount of Olives, across the Kidron Valley from the Old City. The Franciscans maintain the Basilica of the Agony (also called the Church of All Nations) directly adjacent to a grove of ancient olive trees — some verified by dendrochronology to be over a thousand years old, and possibly shoots from roots dating to the time of Christ. The rock inside the basilica, enclosed beneath the high altar, is the traditional site where Christ prayed His agony: "Not my will, but thine."

The basilica's interior is intentionally kept dim — the architect Antonio Barluzzi designed the ceiling mosaics in deep blue and violet so that the light inside echoes nighttime. Pray the relevant passage from the Gospel of Luke (22:39–46) here.

Indulgence: A plenary indulgence is attached to devout prayer at the Rock of the Agony — verify the specific prescribed prayer and conditions with the Franciscan Custody before traveling.

The Mount of Olives

Three sites worth a pilgrim's attention, distinct from Gethsemane:

  • Chapel of the Ascension: A small octagonal shrine controlled by the Waqf (Islamic religious trust) on the summit of the Mount of Olives. On the floor inside is a stone bearing an imprint traditionally identified as the last footprint of Christ before the Ascension. Entry fee required. Christians may celebrate liturgy here with prior arrangement, particularly on Ascension Thursday.
  • Dominus Flevit: The teardrop-shaped Franciscan chapel marking the site where Christ wept over Jerusalem (Dominus flevit — "the Lord wept," Luke 19:41). The east-facing window behind the altar frames the Old City — the Dome of the Rock in the foreground, the Church of the Holy Sepulchre to the left — in what may be the most arresting church view in Christendom.
  • Pater Noster Church / Eleona: The Carmelite church and cloister built over the cave where, by tradition, Christ taught the disciples the Our Father. The text of the Lord's Prayer is displayed on tiles in over 140 languages covering the cloister walls.

Pool of Bethesda / St. Anne's Church

Near the Lion's Gate, just inside the Old City walls. The Basilica of St. Anne is one of the most acoustically perfect Romanesque churches in existence — the Crusader builders, working in the twelfth century, produced something extraordinary. Sing or chant anything inside it and the resonance is remarkable. The church stands over the site of the home of Sts. Joachim and Anne, parents of the Blessed Virgin.

Adjacent are the archaeological excavations of the Pool of Bethesda (John 5:1–15) — the five-porticoed pool where Christ healed the paralytic. You can descend into the ruins. It is not a dramatic or beautiful site; it is a real place where a miracle happened.

The Cenacle (Upper Room)

The traditional site of the Last Supper, the institution of the Eucharist, and the descent of the Holy Ghost at Pentecost — arguably the most theologically loaded room in human history — is controlled by the Israeli government and administered under complex arrangements. The current building is a medieval Crusader hall on Mount Zion, adjacent to the Dormition Abbey. The tomb of King David (a venerated site for Jews) is directly below, which complicates liturgical access.

The current situation is honest: regular Mass cannot be celebrated in the Cenacle without specific governmental permission. Tourist access is available during normal hours, but the pilgrim will find a bare room with no Catholic fixtures, no altar, and a mihrab (Muslim prayer niche) from a period of Ottoman conversion to a mosque. The Franciscans lost custody of the Cenacle in the sixteenth century. They have not given up lobbying for it.

Pray there anyway. Stand in that room. The bare walls do not change what happened there.

Dormition Abbey

On Mount Zion, steps from the Cenacle. The Benedictine Abbey of the Dormition marks the traditional site of the Falling Asleep (dormitio) of the Blessed Virgin Mary — not her tomb (that is in the Garden of Gethsemane, in the Tomb of Mary controlled by the Greek Orthodox), but the house where she died. The crypt contains a recumbent statue of Our Lady in prayer. German Benedictines maintain the abbey and celebrate the Divine Office daily.

Bethlehem

Bethlehem is in the West Bank, under Palestinian Authority administration. You cross an Israeli checkpoint to enter. The process is generally straightforward for tourists with Western passports; allow thirty minutes to an hour.

Basilica of the Nativity / St. Catherine's Church

The oldest continuously used Christian church in the world, built by Constantine in 330 AD (reconstructed by Justinian in the sixth century). The exterior is severe; the entrance doorway has been reduced over the centuries to a low rectangular opening — the Door of Humility — through which every visitor must stoop. This is not accidental. You bow to enter Bethlehem.

The Status Quo applies here as well. The Basilica of the Nativity is divided between the Greek Orthodox (the main nave and the Grotto), the Armenian Apostolic (the rear of the nave), and the Latin Catholics (with primary access through the adjacent St. Catherine's Church). The Grotto of the Nativity — the cave beneath the main altar where Christ was born — is shared. The Star of Bethlehem, a fourteen-pointed silver star set into the marble floor of the Grotto, bears the Latin inscription: Hic de Virgine Maria Iesus Christus natus est — "Here Jesus Christ was born of the Virgin Mary." Touch it. Kiss it if you can reach it through the crowd.

Indulgence: A plenary indulgence is attached to devout veneration at the Star marking the exact site of the Nativity, with the standard conditions — verify the specific prescribed prayers and current grant conditions with the Franciscan Custody before traveling.

St. Catherine's Church (Franciscan, directly adjacent and connected to the Grotto by underground passage) is where the Latin Mass is celebrated. This is your church in Bethlehem. English confessions are available from the Franciscan friars.

Shepherd's Field

Two kilometers east of Bethlehem, near the village of Beit Sahour. The Franciscans maintain one chapel on the site; the Greek Orthodox maintain another. The traditional location where the angels announced the birth of Christ to the shepherds ("Glory to God in the highest"). Underground grottoes on the site may have served as shepherds' shelters. Not a destination for indulgences specifically, but a place of genuine prayer.

Nazareth

Basilica of the Annunciation

The largest church in the Middle East. Built over the Grotto of the Annunciation — the cave where the Angel Gabriel appeared to Mary, and where the Incarnation of the Second Person of the Trinity began. This is not a symbolic site. This is where it happened.

The Franciscans completed the current basilica in 1969 (the design is mid-century modern, which is a matter of taste). The lower level preserves the original Grotto. Pray the Angelus here. It has never been more appropriate.

Indulgence: A plenary indulgence is attached to devout prayer in the Grotto of the Annunciation — verify specific conditions with the Franciscan Custody before traveling.

St. Joseph's Church

A few minutes' walk from the Basilica of the Annunciation, also Franciscan. Built over what tradition identifies as the workshop and home of St. Joseph. The lower level preserves ancient Jewish ritual baths (mikvaot) consistent with a first-century Jewish household.

Galilee

Church of the Multiplication of Loaves and Fishes (Tabgha)

A Benedictine church on the northwest shore of the Sea of Galilee, built over a fourth-century church floor that preserves one of the finest Byzantine mosaics in existence: birds, fish, water plants, and the famous loaves-and-fishes panel beneath the altar. The church marks the site of the feeding of the five thousand. The rock beneath the altar is the traditional stone on which Christ placed the bread and fish before blessing them. Touch it.

Mount of Beatitudes

The hillside overlooking the Sea of Galilee where Christ delivered the Sermon on the Mount. A Franciscan convent and octagonal chapel crown the summit. The view of the lake below, with the Golan Heights to the east, is the same geography Christ looked out at when He said "Blessed are the poor in spirit." Pray the Beatitudes here.

Capernaum

The town of Our Lord's public ministry — "his own city" (Matthew 9:1). The ruins of the synagogue where Christ taught (built in the fourth century over the first-century foundation where He actually preached) are still there. Adjacent is the excavated house of St. Peter, now covered by a modern Franciscan church suspended above the ruins. Here Christ healed Peter's mother-in-law. Here He likely lodged during His Galilean ministry.

The Jordan River / Baptism Site

The traditional site of Christ's baptism by John is Qasr al-Yahud, on the Israeli side of the Jordan, near Jericho. (Bethany beyond the Jordan, on the Jordanian side, is the competing and possibly more archaeologically supported site.) Pilgrims have traditionally renewed their baptismal vows and immersed themselves in the Jordan here. The water is not clean by modern standards; full immersion is your own judgment call.

Indulgence: Renewing baptismal promises at the Jordan River baptism site may carry an indulgence — verify with the Franciscan Custody or the Apostolic Penitentiary before traveling, as the grant language and applicable conditions should be confirmed.

The Franciscan Custody — Your Latin Hosts

The Custodia di Terra Santa has been the Catholic custodian of the Holy Land shrines since 1342. Their website (custodia.org) lists current Mass times and pilgrimage information. Every Franciscan friar you encounter wearing the brown habit in the Old City of Jerusalem, in Bethlehem, in Nazareth, in Galilee, is a guardian of these places in a direct line of succession going back to St. Francis himself, who visited the Holy Land in 1219.

Finding Mass: The Franciscan churches at each major site celebrate daily Mass, predominantly in the morning. The Church of the Holy Sepulchre has Franciscan Masses at the Chapel of the Apparition and the main altars at fixed daily times — confirm current times before arriving, as they shift seasonally and based on shared access agreements under the Status Quo. At Bethlehem, the main Franciscan Mass is at St. Catherine's Church.

Confession in English: Available from Franciscan friars at the Custody's main shrine churches. Ask at the church's sacristy. English-speaking friars are reliably present at the Church of the Holy Sepulchre and St. Catherine's in Bethlehem. For the sacrament outside of these locations, plan ahead — confessors are not always immediately available at every shrine.

Practical Notes

Dress code: Shoulders covered, knees covered, no exceptions. The enforced dress code at the Church of the Holy Sepulchre and the Basilica of the Nativity is strict, and the guards will turn you away. Carry a scarf. Women in chapel veils attract no hostile attention in these churches; they are expected.

Best season: March through May is ideal — mild temperatures, Lent and Easter provide the richest liturgical context, and pilgrimage crowds are manageable outside of Holy Week itself. Holy Week in Jerusalem is extraordinary and overwhelming in equal measure: if you want to pray in peace at the Tomb, do not attempt Holy Saturday morning. September and October offer good weather and thinner crowds. Avoid August — temperatures routinely exceed 100°F, the city is full of tourists rather than pilgrims, and the heat in the Old City streets, which are limestone corridors retaining the sun, is genuinely dangerous.

Christmas at Bethlehem: Midnight Mass at St. Catherine's on Christmas Eve is the Latin rite celebration in Bethlehem, celebrated by the Custos of the Holy Land. Tickets are required and obtained through the Franciscan Custody well in advance. The crowds in Manger Square are large and festive; the Mass itself is beautiful and worth the logistics.

Safety and current situation: The Holy Land is not a simple destination. The Israel-Gaza conflict that escalated in October 2023 dramatically reduced pilgrim traffic; conditions have shifted since and continue to do so. Bethlehem is in the West Bank and subject to the Palestinian Authority; access has historically been reliable for Western pilgrims carrying passports, though Israeli military checkpoint procedures can change. Jerusalem's Old City and Galilee are under Israeli civilian and military control. Standard travel advisories from the U.S. State Department and the Franciscan Custody's own pilgrim communications should be checked before booking. Do not be naively alarmist, but do not be naively optimistic either. Many pilgrims travel there without incident. Research your timing.

The pilgrim who walks the Via Dolorosa, kneels at the Tomb, touches the Star in Bethlehem, and prays in the Grotto of the Annunciation is not doing history tourism with religious extras. He is present at the sites of the Redemption. The stones are real. The events happened. The indulgences attached to these sites exist because the Church recognizes that physical proximity to the loci of salvation — approached with proper dispositions and prescribed prayer — constitutes a genuine meritorious work worthy of the spiritual economy God has established.

Prepare well. Confess. Communicate. Pray for the Pope's intentions. Detach from sin — all of it. Then go.

Terra Sancta te manet.
