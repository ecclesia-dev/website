France: A Pilgrim's Guide to Indulgences & Shrines

March 2, 2026  ·  Ecclesia Dev

France has been called the eldest daughter of the Church, and she has earned the title in blood and stone. Her shrines mark the places where Heaven interrupted history — not once, but repeatedly, and into the modern era. The thread of grace that runs through Lourdes, Chartres, Lisieux, and Paray-le-Monial is still live. These are not dead monuments. They are active dispensaries of grace — if you arrive with the dispositions to receive it.

The Conditions — Always, for Everything

For a plenary indulgence, you need all four:

  1. Sacramental confession — within approximately twenty days before or after the prescribed work
  2. Holy Communion — received on the same day as the indulgenced work
  3. Prayer for the Pope's intentions — an Our Father and Hail Mary suffice
  4. Complete detachment from all sin, including venial — the condition most pilgrims fail without realizing it

Without the fourth condition, a plenary indulgence becomes partial. Many pilgrims confess before leaving home, receive Communion at the shrine, pray the right prayers, and gain a partial indulgence because they are still attached to some habitual sin. Examine your conscience before you leave home, not on the train over.

Lourdes

What It Is

In 1858, the Blessed Virgin Mary appeared eighteen times to Bernadette Soubirous, a fourteen-year-old miller's daughter from a family too poor to afford shoes, at a rubbish-strewn grotto on the edge of the town of Lourdes in the French Pyrenees. She identified herself as the Immaculate Conception — a definition the Church had issued only four years earlier, which the illiterate Bernadette could not have constructed on her own. She directed Bernadette to dig in the mud of the grotto floor; a spring emerged that has flowed ever since. She requested a chapel be built.

The Church investigated and confirmed the apparitions in 1862. What now stands at the Massabielle Grotto is the largest pilgrimage complex in France: the Grotto itself, the underground Basilica of St. Pius X (seating twenty thousand), the Basilica of the Rosary, the upper Basilica of the Immaculate Conception, the baths fed by the miraculous spring, and the Esplanade where the Blessed Sacrament procession moves every evening. Between five and eight million pilgrims come each year.

Bernadette is not buried here. She died as a Sister of Notre Dame de Nevers in Nevers in 1879 and is incorrupt in the convent there — a separate pilgrimage worth making if you are in the region.

What to Do

The Grotto: Begin here. Do not rush to the chapels or the baths first. Stand in front of the rock face where the Virgin appeared. The niche where Our Lady stood is lit and marked above the flowing water. The rosaries pilgrims hang on the iron fencing are the visible record of ten thousand prayers. Light a candle. Pray a Rosary. There is no particular prescribed prayer required for the indulgence — a devout visit with contrition and the standard conditions is what the grant requires.

The Baths: Fed by the miraculous spring, the baths operate every day the shrine is open. You will wait in line, sometimes for hours. You will be led into a small curtained chamber by volunteer helpers (called hospitaliers), undressed to a wet sheet, and briefly immersed in the cold water while they pray with you. Thousands of people use the same water daily; it does not smell of illness. Pilgrims who came paralyzed have left walking. Whether you experience anything dramatic or nothing at all, you are doing what the Virgin asked: you are washing in her spring.

The Blessed Sacrament Procession: Every evening at five o'clock (four-thirty off-season), the Blessed Sacrament is carried in procession from the Grotto across the Esplanade through thousands of pilgrims who came from every nation on earth. Sick pilgrims in wheelchairs and stretchers are arranged in rows at the front. The monstrance passes over them. The crowd chants the Lourdes Hymn in a dozen languages simultaneously. This is not a tourist spectacle. It is the Church alive — ragged, sick, dependent, begging. Be there.

The Stations of the Cross: Carved into the hillside above the shrine complex. The stations are large outdoor bronze sculptures, the path steep and long. Walk them. This is not decoration; the climb itself is the point.

Indulgences

A plenary indulgence is granted to pilgrims who devoutly visit the Sanctuary of Our Lady of Lourdes and pray there, with the standard four conditions fulfilled. The grant is well-established and confirmed. Special jubilee indulgences — with expanded conditions and grants — are published by the Apostolic Penitentiary for major anniversaries; check current publications before traveling. The hospitaliers at the information office can confirm current indulgence conditions.

Chartres

What It Is

The Cathedral of Notre-Dame de Chartres is the greatest Gothic church in France, and possibly in the world — the standard against which every other Gothic cathedral is measured. Construction on the present building began after a fire in 1194 and was largely complete by 1220. The speed of construction is still not fully explained. The result is a unified masterpiece: the same building, the same glass, the same proportions, finished in a generation rather than over centuries like most Gothic cathedrals.

The primary relic is the Sancta Camisia — the tunic or veil said to have been worn by the Virgin Mary at the Annunciation, given to Charlemagne and donated to the cathedral by Charles the Bald in 876. It survived the 1194 fire, which medieval contemporaries interpreted as miraculous and which reignited the construction effort. The relic is displayed in the treasury.

The Chartres Pilgrimage: Every Pentecost weekend, several thousand pilgrims walk seventy miles from Paris to Chartres on foot over three days — the traditional route, organized in a manner that prioritizes actual pilgrimage: Rosaries, sung Masses at road stops, Confessions available throughout, preaching in the evenings. This is one of the most serious acts of penitential pilgrimage available to Catholics today. It is not comfortable, it is not scenic, and that is precisely why it is worth doing. Specific plenary indulgences for the walking pilgrimage are not the primary motivation; the pilgrimage itself is a school of penance and common prayer. Register through the organizing association well in advance.

What to Do

Arrive early in the morning before the tour groups appear. Walk the entire interior slowly. Study the windows — they are the medieval Church's catechism in glass, covering Scripture and hagiography across 176 windows dating from the twelfth and thirteenth centuries. The west rose window, the north rose window, and the south rose window each repay an hour of attention.

The Labyrinth: Set into the nave floor, a medieval walking labyrinth used historically as a penitential substitute for the Jerusalem pilgrimage. Most days it is covered by chairs; visit when it is clear (typically Friday mornings) and walk it in silence.

Venerate the Sancta Camisia in the treasury.

Indulgences

Verify current specific indulgence grants with the cathedral chapter before traveling. Chartres is not a pontifical shrine with the same codified indulgence structure as Lourdes; indulgence grants for cathedral visits are generally governed by their status as minor basilicas or by specific papal grants. The cathedral holds the title of basilica, which carries associated indulgence grants — confirm the current specific conditions with the Diocese of Chartres.

Lisieux

What It Is

St. Thérèse of the Child Jesus died in the Carmelite convent of Lisieux in 1897 at the age of twenty-four. She was canonized in 1925. She is the co-patron of France, a Doctor of the Church, and the author of one of the most theologically serious autobiographies ever written (Story of a Soul), which she completed under obedience while dying of tuberculosis. The little way — spiritual childhood, complete surrender, doing small things with extraordinary love — is a precise theological program, not a sentimental slogan.

The Basilica of St. Thérèse was built after her canonization and completed in 1954; it is large, ornate, and contains her relics. The Carmel of Lisieux, where she lived and died, preserves her cell, the infirmary where she died, and the original cloister. Her parents, Sts. Louis and Zélie Martin, are also canonized; the family home, Les Buissonnets, is preserved as a museum about five minutes' walk from the basilica.

What to Do

Venerate the relics in the basilica. Then go to the Carmel. This is the more serious stop — the place where the sanctity actually happened. The public chapel of the Carmel allows access to the reliquary chapel; the cell and infirmary are visible from the chapel during guided visits. Pray the Office if you can time it for the Hours the Carmelites chant.

Indulgences

A plenary indulgence is granted to pilgrims who devoutly visit the Basilica of St. Thérèse de Lisieux, fulfill the standard conditions, and pray the prescribed prayers. The basilica holds pontifical minor basilica status; verify current specific prescribed prayers and grant conditions with the shrine before traveling.

Paray-le-Monial

What It Is

Between 1673 and 1675, Our Lord appeared repeatedly to Sr. Margaret Mary Alacoque, a Visitation nun in the convent at Paray-le-Monial in Burgundy, revealing His Sacred Heart and requesting the establishment of the Feast of the Sacred Heart, the first Friday devotions, and the Holy Hour of reparation. The apparitions were confirmed; the devotion spread; the Feast was extended to the universal Church in 1856 under Pius IX. The theological content of these apparitions — reparation for the sins of men offered through the pierced Heart of the God-man — is not pious decoration. It is Christology applied to the spiritual life.

The Basilica of the Sacred Heart at Paray-le-Monial is a twelfth-century Cluniac priory church, architecturally significant in its own right. The Chapel of the Apparitions within the adjacent Visitation convent preserves the site of the revelations.

What to Do

Attend Mass at the basilica. Visit the Chapel of the Apparitions. Make the first Friday devotions if your timing permits. Pray the Act of Reparation to the Sacred Heart. This is not primarily a relics-and-indulgences site; it is a site of petition and reparation. Come with something specific to offer.

Indulgences

Verify current indulgence grants with the shrine. Paray-le-Monial has pontifical basilica status; associated grants exist but the specific conditions should be confirmed before traveling.

Vézelay

What It Is

The Basilica of St. Mary Magdalene at Vézelay sits on a hilltop in Burgundy and contains what tradition identifies as the relics of St. Mary Magdalene. The relics' authenticity has always been contested — the rival claim is at Saint-Maximin-la-Sainte-Baume in Provence — but medieval pilgrims did not wait for the controversy to be resolved. Vézelay was one of the four great gathering points for the pilgrimage to Santiago de Compostela; St. Bernard preached the Second Crusade from the basilica steps in 1146; St. Thomas Becket preached from the same site. The building itself — Romanesque, completed in the twelfth century, with one of the finest carved tympana in existence above the central portal — justifies the journey regardless of the relic question.

What to Do

Approach on foot if possible, up the medieval street that climbs to the basilica. The tympanum above the narthex portal — Christ in Majesty sending out the apostles at Pentecost — is the object. Study it before entering. Inside, pray at the crypt containing the relics. The basilica is a UNESCO site and receives visitors who are not pilgrims; pray your Rosary and let the architecture do its catechetical work.

Indulgences

Verify current grants with the basilica. As a basilica with historic pilgrimage status, associated grants exist; the specific conditions should be confirmed before traveling.

Mont Saint-Michel

What It Is

Mont Saint-Michel is not primarily an indulgence site. It is included here because to be a serious Catholic pilgrim in France and skip it is a failure of imagination. A Benedictine abbey on a tidal island off the Normandy coast, dedicated to St. Michael the Archangel by a bishop who claimed a vision in 708. The monastery was fortified, survived the Hundred Years' War, served as a state prison after the Revolution, and was restored to monastic use by the Benedictines of the Jerusalem community in 2001. The abbey church is active. Conventual Mass is celebrated daily.

The island itself — the dramatic profile of the abbey rising from the tidal flats, accessible on foot at low tide across the sand — is one of the great physical spectacles in France. It is also overrun by tourists. Come early in the morning, before the crowds ascend, and attend Mass in the abbey church before the day begins.

Indulgences

No specific codified plenary indulgence grants are attached to Mont Saint-Michel in the ordinary way of major shrines. Verify any current or special grants with the abbey directly. Come for the pilgrimage and the Mass, not the indulgence count.

Rue du Bac, Paris

What It Is

In 1830, in the motherhouse chapel of the Daughters of Charity on Rue du Bac in Paris, the Blessed Virgin Mary appeared to Sr. Catherine Labouré — then a twenty-four-year-old novice — and directed her to have a medal struck bearing a specific image with the prayer: "O Mary, conceived without sin, pray for us who have recourse to thee." The medal spread rapidly across Europe, became known as the Miraculous Medal, and preceded the formal definition of the Immaculate Conception by twenty-four years.

Catherine kept her identity as the visionary secret for forty-six years, telling only her confessor. She died in 1876. The Chapel of Our Lady of the Miraculous Medal on Rue du Bac preserves the original chair in which the Virgin appeared; St. Catherine's incorrupt body is displayed in a side chapel.

What to Do

The chapel is open daily and is run by the Daughters of Charity. Mass is celebrated daily. Attend it if you can time your visit. Venerate the incorrupt body of St. Catherine. Obtain a Miraculous Medal here if you do not already wear one; having it blessed in the chapel where the medal originated is not nothing.

This is Paris. It is easy to arrive as a tourist and leave as one. Resist it. Pray the Memorare before you leave the chapel.

Indulgences

Verify current indulgence grants with the Daughters of Charity at the chapel. The chapel holds pontifical status; associated indulgence grants exist for the devout visit with standard conditions.

Practical Notes

Language: Outside Paris, English is less reliable than in other major European destinations. Carry a phrase book. French Catholics appreciate a pilgrim who makes the effort, even badly. "Je suis en pèlerinage" — "I am on pilgrimage" — opens doors.

Confession: Available in English at Lourdes and Lisieux reliably; at other sites, English-speaking confessors are available but less predictable. Plan accordingly. The Lourdes shrine operates international confessions in the underground chapels near the Grotto almost daily during pilgrimage season.

Season: Lourdes' principal pilgrimage season runs from April through October; the Grotto is accessible year-round but organized pilgrimages and Blessed Sacrament processions are seasonal. The Chartres walk occurs at Pentecost. Mont Saint-Michel is manageable in winter when the summer crowds are absent — the tidal experience in November fog is its own kind of severe.

Priorities: If you can make only one pilgrimage in France, go to Lourdes. The theology of the apparitions, the baths, the processions, the international Church gathered around a sick and suffering pilgrim body — there is nothing quite like it in the world. Chartres is the intellectual complement; Lisieux is the spiritual one. The others are worthy in proportion to your time.

The eldest daughter has prepared a great deal for the pilgrim willing to receive it. The Virgin appeared in France three times in a generation — Rue du Bac, La Salette, Lourdes — and the Church confirmed all three. She was not being idle. She was calling. The pilgrim who arrives at these places prepared, confessed, and resolved for conversion does not leave unchanged.

Fulfill the conditions. Pray for the Pope's intentions. Detach from sin. Go.

Gallia te manet.
