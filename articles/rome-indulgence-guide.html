Rome: A Pilgrim's Guide to Indulgences

February 25, 2026  ·  Ecclesia Dev

Rome is not a tourist destination. It is a treasury. Beneath the traffic and the gelato stands and the selfie-sticks at the Trevi Fountain lies a city soaked in the blood of martyrs, layered over centuries of papal devotion, and threaded through with the spiritual economy of the Church Triumphant interceding for the Church Militant. The pilgrim who comes to Rome for indulgences comes for the most serious business a soul can conduct on earth: the remission of temporal punishment due to sin.

This guide assumes you know what an indulgence is. It assumes you know the conditions. It assumes you are here to work, not sightsee.

The Conditions — Always, for Everything

Before any discussion of churches or routes, fix this in memory:

For a plenary indulgence, you need all four:

  1. Sacramental confession — within approximately twenty days before or after the prescribed work (the Enchiridion uses "about twenty days")
  2. Holy Communion — received on the same day as the indulgenced work
  3. Prayer for the Pope's intentions — an Our Father and a Hail Mary suffice; any approved prayer also suffices
  4. Complete detachment from all sin, including venial — this is the hard one. Without it, the indulgence is partial. Many pilgrims confess, communicate, and do the work, yet gain only a partial indulgence because this fourth condition is not met. Examine your conscience honestly.

If any condition is missing, you gain a partial indulgence. That is not nothing — partial indulgences remit some temporal punishment — but a plenary indulgence wipes the slate. Do not settle.

The Four Major Basilicas

These are the four papal major basilicas of Rome. During Jubilee years, each has a Holy Door. Passing through the Holy Door with the required conditions attached fulfills the Jubilee indulgence. Outside Jubilee years, visiting each basilica, praying there, and fulfilling the standard conditions still earns a plenary indulgence — the specific prescribed work varies but typically involves a pious visit with the prescribed prayers.

St. Peter's Basilica (Basilica di San Pietro)

The tomb of the Prince of the Apostles. Everything else in Christendom is oriented, at least culturally, toward this point. Built over the Circus of Nero where Peter was crucified, the current Renaissance edifice replaced the original Constantinian basilica in the sixteenth century. Michelangelo's dome. Bernini's baldachin. The Pietà behind bullet-proof glass, courtesy of a madman with a hammer in 1972.

What to do: Enter through the central nave. The Chapel of the Blessed Sacrament (Cappella del Santissimo Sacramento) is on the right side — this is where you pray, not the tourist areas. The Tomb of St. Peter is beneath the main altar; a descent to the Grotte Vaticane (Vatican Grottoes) brings you to the tombs of the popes and closer to the Apostle's actual grave. English confessions are available in the confessionals along the nave — look for the language signs posted on each confessional.

Practical: Open daily 7:00am–7:00pm (April–September), 7:00am–6:30pm (October–March). Dress code is strictly enforced at the entrance — shoulders and knees covered, no exceptions. There is no entry fee for the basilica itself; the dome and Grottoes charge separately. Metro Line A to Ottaviano or Cipro, then a short walk.

St. John Lateran (Arcibasilica di San Giovanni in Laterano)

"Mother and head of all churches of the city and the world" — this inscription on the facade is not boasting. The Lateran is the Pope's cathedral as Bishop of Rome. St. Peter's belongs to the universal Church; the Lateran is where the Pope sits as a local bishop. The distinction matters. Constantine gave the Lateran to Pope Miltiades in 313, and papal residence remained there until the Avignon exile. It is older, in terms of continuous papal presence, than anything at the Vatican.

What to do: The high altar enshrines relics of the heads of Sts. Peter and Paul — an extraordinary thing to contemplate while praying at the foot of that altar. The apse mosaic is medieval in origin (heavily restored under Leo XIII). During Jubilee years, the Holy Door is on the far right of the facade. Outside Jubilee years, the prescribed pious visit with devotional prayer earns the attached plenary indulgence.

Practical: Open daily 7:00am–6:30pm. Dress code enforced. English confessions available; check the board near the entrance for scheduled times or ask the sacristan. Metro Line A to San Giovanni.

Adjacent — the Scala Sancta: Do not miss this. Directly across the piazza from the Lateran stands the Pontifical Sanctuary of the Holy Stairs (Scala Sancta) — twenty-eight marble steps which tradition holds are the stairs Christ ascended to stand before Pilate at the praetorium in Jerusalem, brought to Rome by St. Helena in the fourth century. You ascend them only on your knees, praying on each step. The stairs lead to the Sancta Sanctorum — "Holy of Holies" — the ancient private chapel of the popes, housing an acheiropoieton icon of Christ (not made by human hands, attributed to St. Luke with angelic completion). A plenary indulgence is attached to ascending the Scala Sancta on one's knees, with the standard conditions. The surrounding lateral staircases are for descent and non-penitential ascent. Open daily 6:00am–1:00pm and 3:00pm–7:00pm (summer); shorter hours in winter — confirm before visiting.

St. Mary Major (Basilica di Santa Maria Maggiore)

The greatest Marian church in Rome, and the only major basilica that retains a substantially original interior from late antiquity — the fifth-century nave mosaics are intact. Legend holds it was built on the site where snow fell miraculously in August on the Esquiline Hill at the direction of Our Lady, establishing the footprint of the church. The feast of Our Lady of the Snows (August 5) commemorates this. Each year on that date, white flower petals are released from the Borghese Chapel ceiling.

What to do: The high altar enshrines a relic of the Holy Crib (Sacra Culla) — timber traditionally held to be from the manger at Bethlehem. The Borghese Chapel (Pauline Chapel, on the left) houses the icon Salus Populi Romani — "Health of the Roman People" — a Byzantine icon of Our Lady, one of the most venerated images in Rome. This is where you pray. Pope Francis begins each significant journey in Rome here. The tomb of St. Pius V is in the Sistine Chapel of this basilica (right side) — appropriate for a traditional pilgrim to honor.

Practical: Open daily 7:00am–7:00pm. Dress code enforced. English confessions available. Metro Line A to Termini or Repubblica; also reachable on foot from the Lateran in about twenty minutes.

St. Paul Outside the Walls (Basilica di San Paolo fuori le Mura)

The most austere of the four, and the most dramatic upon entry — a massive five-nave basilica rebuilt after the devastating fire of 1823 destroyed most of the ancient structure. The current church is nineteenth-century in fabric but follows the original Constantinian plan. The high altar stands directly over the tomb of the Apostle Paul, confirmed by Vatican archaeological investigations in 2009. A small window in the altar allows a view down to the tomb.

What to do: Stand at the confessio. Pray ad limina apostolorum — at the threshold of the Apostle. The medallion portraits of every pope since St. Peter ring the nave; when that sequence fills, tradition holds the end of the age is near (currently there are a handful of spaces remaining). The attached Benedictine monastery garden is open to visitors and provides rare quiet. English confessions available; the attached Benedictine community maintains regular hours.

Practical: Open daily 7:00am–6:30pm. Dress code enforced. Located outside the old Aurelian walls, south of the city center — Metro Line B to Basilica San Paolo. Not walkable from the other three major basilicas without significant effort.

The Seven Churches Pilgrimage (Le Sette Chiese)

Around 1553, Philip Neri, devoting himself to works of charity in Rome, began organizing nocturnal pilgrimages to the seven ancient pilgrim churches. The walk was done on the night before Ash Wednesday, departing after dark, arriving at each church for prayers and a brief exhortation by Neri, sharing a meal in the gardens of the Villa Mattei with bread, wine, cheese, and singing, and completing the circuit by morning. It was explicitly designed as a counter-program to the debauchery of Carnivale. It became enormously popular and is considered one of the seeds of what became the Oratory.

The route (classic order, roughly 12–14 miles on foot):

  1. San Sebastiano fuori le Mura — Appian Way, over the catacombs bearing his name
  2. San Lorenzo fuori le Mura — Basilica over the tomb of the deacon-martyr Lawrence
  3. Santa Croce in Gerusalemme — Houses relics of the True Cross, including a portion of the Titulus (the inscription from the Cross), brought by St. Helena
  4. San Giovanni in Laterano — The Pope's cathedral
  5. Santa Maria Maggiore — The great Marian basilica
  6. San Pietro in Vaticano — St. Peter's
  7. San Paolo fuori le Mura — The final station, at the tomb of the Apostle to the Gentiles

The walk is traditionally done on the Tuesday night before Ash Wednesday (overnight into Wednesday morning), though organized pilgrimages now happen throughout the year. Many pilgrims today walk sections and take public transport for others — there is no obligation to walk the full route on foot for the indulgence to apply, though the mortification of the walk has obvious value.

The indulgence: A plenary indulgence is attached to completing the Seven Churches pilgrimage with the standard conditions. This has been confirmed repeatedly by papal authority. The key is pious visitation of each church with prescribed prayer.

Other Notable Roman Indulgences

Santa Maria in Aracoeli (Capitoline Hill)

The ancient Franciscan church atop the Capitoline — traditionally built on the site where Augustus received the Sibyl's prophecy of a coming King. Houses the Santo Bambino, a venerated olive-wood figure of the Christ Child reportedly carved from an olive tree in the Garden of Gethsemane (the original was stolen in 1994; a copy now occupies the chapel). Devotion to the Santo Bambino, particularly around Christmas, draws large numbers of Roman faithful. Whether a specific plenary indulgence is attached to the Santo Bambino devotion here beyond standard Franciscan third-order privileges — verify before traveling, as the current status under the revised Enchiridion Indulgentiarum (1999) should be confirmed with the Apostolic Penitentiary or the basilica directly.

The Portiuncula Indulgence

The Portiuncula indulgence — one of the most generous in the Church — is properly attached to the chapel of the Porziuncola inside the Basilica of Santa Maria degli Angeli in Assisi, not in Rome. It was granted by Our Lord Himself to St. Francis and confirmed by Honorius III in 1216. Available August 1–2 each year, and extended since to any properly designated Franciscan or parish church. If you are traveling to Rome, Assisi is a day-trip by train (approximately two and a half hours from Rome Termini). Do not miss it.

Practical Notes

Best time to visit: Holy Week and the weeks immediately surrounding it offer the richest liturgical context. Crowds are significant but pilgrims are among pilgrims. September and October offer better weather and thinner tourist crowds while maintaining normal operations. Avoid August if heat is a concern — Rome in August is genuinely oppressive, and the Eternal City empties of Romans.

Jubilee years: In a Jubilee year (most recently 2025), the Holy Doors of the four major basilicas are opened, and passing through them while fulfilling the standard conditions earns the Jubilee plenary indulgence. The Jubilee also typically brings enhanced confession availability and expanded indulgences attached to various works. Check the Vatican website for current-year specifics.

Dress code: Shoulders covered, knees covered, no exceptions at any of the four major basilicas. The guards at St. Peter's will turn you away. Keep a scarf in your bag. Women in chapel veils will not attract hostile attention in any of these churches — they are more common in Rome than in most American parishes.

Confession in English:

  • St. Peter's: Confessionals in the nave with language signs; English confessors are typically available throughout the day
  • St. John Lateran: English available; check the sacristan's board for scheduled times
  • St. Mary Major: English available; the Redemptorist Fathers at the nearby Sant'Alfonso hear confessions extensively in English
  • San Silvestro in Capite (near the Trevi Fountain): Staffed by English-speaking Pallottine Fathers; confessions in English daily — one of the best-kept practical secrets for Anglophone pilgrims
  • Santa Susanna (American church of Rome): Confessions in English; worth knowing the location (near the American Embassy)

A word on preparation: The most common failure mode of the Roman pilgrimage is treating it as a checklist. Walk into each church. Stop. Kneel. Remember why you are there. The city will try to overwhelm you with its own grandeur; the supernatural treasure beneath it is what you came for.

Roma locuta est.
