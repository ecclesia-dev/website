# What Is an Indulgence?

Few Catholic doctrines are more misunderstood than the indulgence. Protestants think it is a license to sin. Poorly catechized Catholics think it is something the Church stopped doing after the Middle Ages. Neither is true. The Church grants indulgences today, the doctrine is unchanged, and it has nothing to do with buying or selling forgiveness.

An indulgence is the remission before God of the temporal punishment due to sin, the guilt of which has already been forgiven. That sentence is the entire doctrine. Read it again. The sin is already forgiven — through the Sacrament of Confession. What remains is the punishment, and it is this punishment that the indulgence remits, wholly or in part.

## Sin, Guilt, and Punishment

To understand indulgences, you must understand the Catholic teaching on the consequences of sin. When a man commits a mortal sin, two things happen: he incurs guilt, and he incurs punishment. Absolution in Confession removes the guilt and the eternal punishment (Hell). But temporal punishment remains. This is not arbitrary; it is the nature of justice. A man who sets fire to his neighbor's house may be forgiven by his neighbor, but the house is still burned. Restitution is owed. The disorder introduced by sin must be repaired.

This temporal punishment is satisfied in one of three ways: by penance in this life (prayer, fasting, almsgiving, suffering accepted in union with Christ), by purification in Purgatory after death, or by an indulgence granted by the Church.

## Partial and Plenary

An indulgence is either partial or plenary. A partial indulgence remits some of the temporal punishment due. A plenary indulgence remits all of it — every last debt of temporal punishment on the soul at the moment the indulgence is gained. If a man were to die immediately after gaining a plenary indulgence with perfect dispositions, he would go straight to Heaven, with no passage through Purgatory.

The current norms for indulgences are found in the *Enchiridion Indulgentiarum* (1999), the Church's official handbook. Partial indulgences are attached to many prayers and pious acts — the Rosary, the Angelus, spiritual reading of Scripture, and many others. Plenary indulgences are granted for specific acts performed under specific conditions.

## The Four Conditions for a Plenary Indulgence

The *Enchiridion* requires four conditions, all of which must be fulfilled:

**First**, sacramental Confession — within about twenty days before or after the indulgenced act. A single Confession suffices for multiple plenary indulgences, provided the other conditions are met each time.

**Second**, Holy Communion — received on the day the indulgence is sought, or within several days before or after. Unlike Confession, each plenary indulgence requires a separate Communion.

**Third**, prayer for the intentions of the Holy Father — an Our Father and a Hail Mary suffice, though other prayers may be substituted.

**Fourth**, complete detachment from all sin, including venial sin. This is the hardest condition. If any attachment to any sin remains, the indulgence is partial, not plenary. This is the safeguard built into the system: an indulgence cannot be gained mechanically. The interior disposition matters absolutely.

**Note**: Only one plenary indulgence may be gained per day, except at the hour of death (*Enchiridion Indulgentiarum*, norm 18 §1). A partial indulgence may be gained multiple times per day unless otherwise stated.

## The Treasury of the Church

The theological foundation of indulgences is the Treasury of the Church — the infinite merits of Christ and the superabundant merits of the Blessed Virgin and the saints. Christ's satisfaction for sin is infinite; it exceeds what is needed for the redemption of the whole human race. The saints, united to Christ, have likewise offered penances and sufferings beyond what was required for their own sanctification. These merits do not sit idle. They constitute a spiritual treasury from which the Church, as steward of the means of salvation, can draw on behalf of her members.

This is not a metaphor. Pope Clement VI defined the doctrine in *Unigenitus Dei Filius* (1343), and the Council of Trent reaffirmed the Church's authority to grant indulgences from this treasury (Session XXV).

## The 16th-Century Abuse and the Protestant Distortion

What happened in the 16th century was not a failure of doctrine but a failure of discipline. Certain preachers — most notoriously Johann Tetzel — attached the granting of indulgences to financial contributions in ways that were pastorally reckless and, in some cases, canonically irregular. The formula attributed to Tetzel — "As soon as the coin in the coffer rings, the soul from Purgatory springs" — was a distortion, not an official teaching. The actual papal bull authorizing the St. Peter's indulgence required the same interior dispositions as any other indulgence. Tetzel simplified, exaggerated, and thereby scandalized.

Luther's response was to reject not only the abuse but the doctrine itself — and with it, the entire theology of merit, satisfaction, Purgatory, and the communion of saints. This was not reform but demolition. The Council of Trent, in its final session (Session XXV, 1563), affirmed the doctrine of indulgences, condemned the abuses, and ordered that "all evil gains for the obtaining of them be wholly abolished." The discipline was corrected. The doctrine stood.

## Indulgences Today

Paul VI reformed the indulgence system in 1967 with the apostolic constitution *Indulgentiarum Doctrina*, simplifying the categories (the old system of "days" and "years" was removed) and emphasizing interior conversion. The *Enchiridion Indulgentiarum* of 1999 further codified the norms. The system is cleaner, simpler, and no less real than it was in the 13th century.

Indulgences are also applicable to the dead — offered *per modum suffragii*, by way of intercession. You cannot gain a plenary indulgence for yourself and transfer it to a deceased soul at will; rather, you offer the indulgence to God on behalf of a soul in Purgatory, and God applies it according to His justice and mercy. This is one of the most powerful acts of charity available to any Catholic, and one of the least practiced.

The doctrine of indulgences is not medieval. It is not obsolete. It is the Church exercising, as she has always exercised, the power of the keys given to Peter: "Whatsoever thou shalt loose upon earth, it shall be loosed also in heaven" (Matt. 16:19, DRB). Use them. Gain them. Offer them for the dead. The Treasury is open, and the souls in Purgatory are waiting.
