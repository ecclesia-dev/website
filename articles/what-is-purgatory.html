What Is Purgatory?

March 3, 2026  ·  Ecclesia Dev

Purgatory is the state in which souls who die in the grace of God undergo purification before entering into the Beatific Vision. It is not a second chance — it is the final preparation of the saved.

Purgatory is the state in which souls who die in the grace of God undergo purification before entering into the Beatific Vision. It is not a “second chance.” It is not a middle ground between Heaven and Hell. It is the final preparation of the saved — those who have been forgiven but who still owe a debt of temporal punishment, or who retain some disordered attachment to created things that must be burned away before they can see God face to face.

The existence of Purgatory is defined dogma. Denying it is heresy. The Council of Florence (1439), the Council of Trent (Session XXV, 1563), and the constant ordinary Magisterium of the Church all affirm it. This is not a pious opinion or a medieval invention; it is a truth of the faith.

The Two Elements

Catholic theology identifies two aspects of the purification that takes place in Purgatory.

First, the satisfaction of temporal punishment due to sin already forgiven. When a man confesses his sins and receives absolution, the guilt is removed and the eternal punishment (Hell) is remitted. But temporal punishment often remains. This is the justice of God — real but remedial. David was forgiven his sin with Bathsheba, yet the child still died (2 Kings 12:13–14). Forgiveness does not erase every consequence.

Second, the purification of remaining attachment to sin. A man may die in the state of grace without mortal sin on his soul, yet still carry within himself a disordered love of creatures — a residual clinging to comfort, vanity, self-will. This attachment is incompatible with the Beatific Vision. “There shall not enter into it any thing defiled” (Apoc. 21:27, DRB). Purgatory removes the defilement, not the person.

The Scriptural Basis

The Protestant objection that Purgatory is “not in the Bible” fails on multiple counts.

The most direct text is 2 Maccabees 12:46: “It is therefore a holy and wholesome thought to pray for the dead, that they may be loosed from sins.” If the dead are either in Heaven (needing no prayers) or in Hell (beyond help), then prayer for the dead is pointless. But the inspired author calls it “holy and wholesome.” There must be a third state — and that state is Purgatory. Protestants removed 2 Maccabees from their canon, but the Catholic Church received it as inspired Scripture at the Councils of Hippo (393), Carthage (397), and definitively at Trent.

St. Paul writes to the Corinthians: “If any man's work burn, he himself shall be saved, yet so as by fire” (1 Cor. 3:15, DRB). Saved — but through fire. Not damned, but purified. The Fathers read this text as referring to a purgatorial fire, and the Church has consistently upheld this interpretation.

Our Lord Himself says: “Thou shalt not go out from thence till thou repay the last farthing” (Matt. 5:26, DRB). The prison from which one eventually emerges is neither Heaven (from which no one departs) nor Hell (from which no one escapes). It is Purgatory.

What We Know and What We Don't

The Church defines the fact of Purgatory, not the details. The Council of Trent decreed that Purgatory exists and that the souls detained there are helped by the suffrages of the faithful, “especially the acceptable Sacrifice of the Altar.” Beyond this, the Church has not defined the nature of the fire, the duration of the suffering, or the precise conditions of the purgatorial state.

Theologians have speculated — some hold that the fire is real and corporeal, others that it is metaphorical for the soul's intense longing for God. St. Catherine of Genoa wrote movingly of purgatorial suffering as an act of love — the soul willingly enduring the pain because it knows the purification is bringing it closer to God. These are permissible opinions, not binding doctrine. What is binding is that Purgatory exists, that it is painful, and that it ends in Heaven.

Our Duty to the Holy Souls

Praying for the dead is not optional. It is one of the spiritual works of mercy — and arguably the most neglected. The souls in Purgatory can no longer merit for themselves. They depend entirely on the prayers, sacrifices, and indulgences offered by the living. They are members of the Body of Christ, suffering in union with Him, and they need our help.

The most powerful suffrage is the Holy Sacrifice of the Mass offered for the repose of a soul. Having Masses said for the dead is an ancient practice — attested from the earliest centuries — and it remains the single most effective thing you can do for a deceased loved one. Beyond this: the Rosary, the De Profundis (Psalm 129), the Heroic Act of Charity (by which one offers all personal indulgences and satisfactory works for the souls in Purgatory), and the gaining of indulgences applicable to the dead per modum suffragii.

The month of November is dedicated to the Holy Souls, and the Church grants a plenary indulgence (under the usual conditions) for visiting a cemetery and praying for the dead from November 1–8. But do not limit your charity to November. The holy souls need your prayers every day.

Against the Denials

The Protestant denial of Purgatory rests on the claim that Christ's sacrifice is “sufficient” and therefore nothing remains to be done after forgiveness. But this confuses sufficiency with application. Christ's sacrifice is infinitely sufficient — no Catholic disputes this. The question is how its merits are applied to individual souls. The Church teaches that they are applied through the sacraments, through prayer, through penance, and through indulgences. Purgatory is part of this economy of salvation, not a contradiction of it.

Annihilationism — the view that the unsaved simply cease to exist — is equally incompatible with Catholic teaching. The soul is immortal. It does not perish. It goes somewhere. For those who die in grace but imperfectly purified, that somewhere is Purgatory, and from Purgatory they will proceed to the Heaven for which God created them.

Pray for the dead. Offer Masses. Gain indulgences. The holy souls are suffering, and they cannot help themselves. You can help them — and when they reach Heaven, they will not forget you.
