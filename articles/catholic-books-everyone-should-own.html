Catholic Books Every Serious Catholic Should Own

March 3, 2026  ·  Ecclesia Dev

A Catholic without books is a Catholic without roots. The Faith is not a feeling — it is a body of doctrine, a tradition of prayer, a history of sanctity stretching back two thousand years. All of it has been written down. If you have not read the major works, you are living off secondhand summaries and homilies that may or may not be sound.

What follows is not a browsing list. These are books you should own, physically, in your home. Read them. Return to them. Mark them up. A Catholic library is not a collection; it is an arsenal.

Scripture

Douay-Rheims Bible (1899 Challoner Revision)

The standard English Catholic Bible, translated from the Latin Vulgate — the text the Church declared authoritative at Trent. The Challoner revision modernized the English while preserving the translation's fidelity to the Vulgate. Modern translations are made from critical Greek and Hebrew texts that the Church never formally adopted; the Douay-Rheims is translated from the text the Church actually uses. Available from Baronius Press in a quality hardcover edition, or from TAN Books. Get a copy with the original annotations.

Catechism

Baltimore Catechism No. 3

Written for the Third Plenary Council of Baltimore (1884) and used to catechize American Catholics for nearly a century. The question-and-answer format is precise, systematic, and comprehensive. No. 3 is the full adult version — not the simplified children's editions (Nos. 1 and 2). Every doctrine is stated clearly, with explanations and scriptural references. If you were poorly catechized — and statistically, you were — start here. Available from TAN Books.

Catechism of the Council of Trent (Roman Catechism)

Commissioned by the Council of Trent and published in 1566, this is the catechism the Church wrote for parish priests. It is organized around the Creed, the Sacraments, the Commandments, and the Our Father. The depth of treatment far exceeds any modern catechism. Where the Baltimore Catechism gives you the answer, the Roman Catechism gives you the theology behind the answer. Available from TAN Books (the McHugh and Callan translation is standard).

Spiritual Classics

The Imitation of Christ — Thomas à Kempis

The most widely read Christian book after Sacred Scripture. Written in the fifteenth century by an Augustinian canon, it is a manual for interior conversion — direct, severe, and unflinching. It assumes you are serious about sanctity and treats every compromise with the world as a betrayal of Christ. Read a chapter a day. The Aloysius Croft and Harold Bolton translation is reliable. Available from multiple publishers; the Image Books edition is inexpensive and widely available.

Introduction to the Devout Life — St. Francis de Sales

Written in 1609 for laypeople living in the world — not monks, not nuns, but ordinary Catholics trying to pursue holiness amid secular obligations. De Sales is gentle, practical, and precise. He covers prayer, the sacraments, temptation, friendship, and the ordering of daily life. If the Imitation is the hammer, the Introduction is the blueprint. The John K. Ryan translation (Image Books) is standard.

The Story of a Soul — St. Thérèse of Lisieux

The autobiography of the Little Flower, who entered Carmel at fifteen and died at twenty-four. Her "Little Way" — doing small things with great love, offering every suffering to God — is not sentimental. It is a theology of spiritual childhood that Pius X called "the greatest saint of modern times." The ICS Publications critical edition is the most complete. Available from TAN Books as well.

True Devotion to the Blessed Virgin Mary — St. Louis de Montfort

The foundational text of Marian consecration. De Montfort argues that the shortest, surest path to Christ is through total consecration to Mary — giving her everything: merits, prayers, actions, sufferings. The theology is rigorous, rooted in the Fathers, and has been endorsed by every pope who has commented on it. St. John Paul II took his papal motto (Totus Tuus) from this book. Available from TAN Books. Do the 33-day preparation.

Theology

Summa Theologica — St. Thomas Aquinas

The greatest work of systematic theology ever written. The entire structure of Catholic doctrine is laid out in questions, objections, and replies — with a precision that has never been equaled. You do not need to read all of it at once. Start with the Prima Pars (the existence and nature of God, the angels, creation) and the Prima Secundae and Secunda Secundae (the moral life, the virtues, the vices). The Benziger Brothers edition (1947), translated by the English Dominican Fathers, is the standard. Available from multiple publishers. The complete text is also available free online at newadvent.org.

The Mystical City of God — Ven. Mary of Agreda

A four-volume mystical revelation of the life of the Blessed Virgin Mary, received by the seventeenth-century Spanish Franciscan nun. It is extraordinarily detailed, theologically dense, and devotionally powerful. The Church has investigated it multiple times and never condemned it; several popes have praised it. Not for beginners — read it after you have a solid grounding in doctrine. The Fiscar Marison translation is standard. Available from TAN Books.

History and Apologetics

The Faith of Our Fathers — Cardinal James Gibbons

Published in 1876 and one of the most successful works of Catholic apologetics ever written in English. Gibbons addresses every major Protestant objection — Scripture alone, the papacy, the sacraments, purgatory, devotion to Mary — with clarity, charity, and devastating precision. Over two million copies sold. Available from TAN Books.

How the Catholic Church Built Western Civilization — Thomas E. Woods Jr.

A corrective to the modern myth that the Church was an obstacle to progress. Woods documents the Church's foundational role in the development of universities, science, law, economics, art, and morality. Well-sourced, readable, and essential for any Catholic who needs to answer the claim that the Church held civilization back. Available from Regnery Publishing.

Devotional

The Roman Breviary (1961 Edition)

The Divine Office — the official prayer of the Church, prayed daily by every priest and religious. The 1961 edition is the last pre-reform Breviary, containing the full cycle of psalms, hymns, antiphons, and readings that structured Catholic prayer for centuries. Laypeople are not obligated to pray it, but those who do enter into the Church's own prayer at every hour of the day. The Baronius Press edition is the standard Latin-English version. It is not cheap. It is worth it.

My Prayer Book — Fr. F.X. Lasance

The finest English-language Catholic prayer manual ever published. Lasance compiled prayers for every occasion — morning and evening, before and after Mass, for the sick, for the dying, for the holy souls, for every feast and season. First published in 1908, it has never been surpassed. Available from Angelus Press and Baronius Press. Keep it at your home altar.

How to Build the Library

Do not buy everything at once. Start with the Douay-Rheims, the Baltimore Catechism, the Imitation of Christ, and True Devotion. Read those first. Then add the rest, one or two at a time, as your understanding deepens.

Buy physical copies. Digital is useful for searching, but you will not form a relationship with a PDF. Books that matter should be in your hands, on your shelf, with your notes in the margins.

The principal sources: TAN Books (tanbooks.com), Baronius Press (baroniuspress.com), Angelus Press (angeluspress.com). Used copies are often available through AbeBooks and other secondhand dealers.

A Catholic home without serious books is a home without depth. Build the library. Read the books. The Faith is not a slogan — it is a tradition, and traditions are transmitted through texts.
