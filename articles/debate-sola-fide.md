# Sola Fide: A Formal Disputation

## A structured debate between the Reformed and Catholic positions on justification by faith alone

The doctrine of *Sola Fide* — justification by faith alone — is the article on which the Reformation stood or fell. Luther called it the doctrine by which the Church stands or falls. Calvin built his entire soteriology upon it. The Catholic Church condemned it at the Council of Trent. What follows is a formal disputation between these two positions, presented at their strongest, because the truth has nothing to fear from a fair fight.

---

## Exchange 1 — The Proposition

**CALVIN:**

The Gospel of Jesus Christ is this: that sinners are justified before God not by any works they perform, not by any inherent righteousness infused into their souls, but solely by faith in Christ, whose perfect righteousness is imputed to them by a forensic act of divine declaration. This is the heart of the Reformation, and it is the heart of the Gospel itself.

St. Paul is unambiguous: "Therefore we conclude that a man is justified by faith without the deeds of the law" (Rom. 3:28). Again: "Knowing that a man is not justified by the works of the law, but by the faith of Jesus Christ, even we have believed in Jesus Christ, that we might be justified by the faith of Christ, and not by the works of the law: for by the works of the law shall no flesh be justified" (Gal. 2:16). The contrast is total. Faith stands on one side; works of every kind stand on the other. Justification belongs to faith.

What is justification? It is not a process of interior renovation. It is a legal verdict. God the righteous Judge declares the sinner righteous — not because the sinner has become righteous, but because the righteousness of Christ has been credited to his account. As our Westminster Confession states: "Those whom God effectually calleth, he also freely justifieth; not by infusing righteousness into them, but by pardoning their sins, and by accounting and accepting their persons as righteous; not for anything wrought in them, or done by them, but for Christ's sake alone; not by imputing faith itself, the act of believing, or any other evangelical obedience to them, as their righteousness; but by imputing the obedience and satisfaction of Christ unto them" (Westminster Confession, XI.1).

This is the great exchange: our sin laid upon Christ at the Cross, His righteousness credited to us by faith. The believer contributes nothing — not his love, not his works, not his cooperation. He receives. That is what faith is: an empty hand that receives the gift. To add anything to faith as a condition of justification is to overthrow grace entirely. "And if by grace, then is it no more of works: otherwise grace is no more grace" (Rom. 11:6). The moment you make justification contingent upon the sinner's own transformation, you have placed man's salvation back upon man's shoulders, and you have robbed God of His glory. This is not a secondary doctrine. It is the difference between a religion of grace and a religion of merit. It is the article upon which the Church stands or falls.

**BELLARMINE:**

The Reformed position sounds noble in the abstract, but it does not survive contact with the full testimony of Scripture, the Fathers, or the Apostolic Tradition. Let us begin with what the Catholic Church actually teaches.

The Council of Trent defines justification as "not remission of sins merely, but also the sanctification and renewal of the inward man, through the voluntary reception of the grace and of the gifts, whereby man of unjust becomes just" (Trent, Session VI, Chapter VII). Justification is not a legal fiction. It is a real transformation wrought by God in the soul. When God declares a man just, He makes him just. God does not lie. His word accomplishes what it signifies. When He said "Let there be light," there was light. When He declares a sinner righteous, the sinner becomes righteous — not by his own power, but by the grace poured into his soul through the Holy Spirit. "The charity of God is poured forth in our hearts, by the Holy Ghost, who is given to us" (Rom. 5:5, DRB).

Now to Calvin's reading of Romans 3:28. Paul writes that a man is justified by faith "without the deeds of the law" — *choris ergon nomou*. He does not write "by faith alone." The word "alone" (*sola*, *monon*) is not in the Greek text. Luther famously inserted it into his German translation and defended the addition by appealing to the demands of the German language. But the demands of the German language do not override the words of the Holy Spirit. And lest there be any doubt, the Holy Spirit Himself addresses this question directly. St. James writes: "Do you see that by works a man is justified, and not by faith only?" (James 2:24, DRB). *Not by faith only.* This is the one place in all of Scripture where the words "faith" and "alone" appear together in relation to justification — and they appear in a negation. The Reformed position must explain away the one verse that directly addresses its central claim.

Furthermore, justification by imputation alone — a purely forensic verdict with no interior change — reduces God's saving act to a bookkeeping entry. The sinner remains inwardly corrupt but is pronounced righteous by a courtroom decree. This is what the Fathers called a *nuda imputatio*, a naked imputation, and it is foreign to the entire Patristic tradition. God does not merely cover sin; He destroys it. He does not merely declare; He transforms. "If then any be in Christ a new creature, the old things are passed away. Behold all things are made new" (2 Cor. 5:17, DRB). This is not a change of legal status. It is a change of being.

## Exchange 2 — The Fathers

**CALVIN:**

I will not yield Augustine to Rome without a fight. Augustine is the Doctor of Grace, and his doctrine of grace is far closer to the Reformed position than modern Catholicism would like to admit. Augustine taught that man after the Fall is utterly incapable of any good apart from the sovereign grace of God. He taught that grace is not merely offered but effectual — that God does not merely make salvation possible but actually accomplishes it in His elect. "It is certain that it is we that will when we will, but it is He that makes us will what is good" (*De Gratia et Libero Arbitrio*, XVII.33). This is the heart of Reformed soteriology: the will is enslaved to sin until God frees it by irresistible grace.

Against the Pelagians, Augustine insisted that even the beginning of faith is a gift of God, not a human contribution. "What hast thou that thou didst not receive?" (1 Cor. 4:7). The entire trajectory of Augustine's anti-Pelagian works — *On the Spirit and the Letter*, *On Grace and Free Will*, *On the Predestination of the Saints* — drives toward the conclusion that salvation is wholly of God and in no way contingent upon human cooperation as a meritorious cause. When Augustine writes that God "crowns His own gifts" when He crowns our merits, he is not affirming the Tridentine system of condign merit. He is saying precisely what the Reformed say: that every good thing in the believer comes from God alone and cannot be the basis of a boast.

Turretin rightly observed that the Fathers must be read in their polemical context. When Augustine speaks of merit, he is combating Pelagius, not constructing the elaborate merit-theology of the medieval schoolmen. His fundamental principle — that grace precedes, accompanies, and completes every good work — is the Reformed principle. We do not deny that the believer does good works. We deny that those works form any part of the ground of justification. And on this point, Augustine is our witness: "By grace you have been saved through faith; and that not of yourselves, for it is the gift of God; not of works, that no man may glory" (Eph. 2:8–9, as cited repeatedly in Augustine's anti-Pelagian corpus).

**BELLARMINE:**

Calvin reads Augustine the way a man reads a menu — selecting what appeals and ignoring the rest. Let us have the full Augustine, not the excerpted Augustine.

Yes, Augustine taught the absolute priority of grace. On this the Catholic Church agrees entirely, and Trent affirmed it: "None of those things which precede justification — whether faith or works — merit the grace itself of justification. For, if it be a grace, it is not now by works; otherwise, as the same Apostle says, grace is no more grace" (Trent, Session VI, Chapter VIII). Rome does not teach Pelagianism. Rome does not teach that man initiates his own salvation. The Council of Orange (529), which Augustine's own disciples shaped, condemned Semi-Pelagianism and was affirmed by Pope Boniface II. Calvin's argument depends on the fiction that Catholic theology teaches what Catholic theology has formally condemned.

But now let us read what Calvin omits. Augustine believed in the Real Presence of Christ in the Eucharist: "That Bread which you see on the altar, having been sanctified by the word of God, is the Body of Christ" (*Sermon 227*). Augustine prayed for his dead mother Monica: "I pour out unto Thee, our God, on her behalf, my tears... And inspire, O Lord my God, inspire Thy servants my brethren... that so many as shall read these confessions may at Thy altar remember Monica" (*Confessions*, IX.13.36–37). Prayer for the dead presupposes Purgatory — a doctrine the Reformed reject. Augustine affirmed that works done in grace are truly meritorious: "He deigned to make their merits His gifts" (*De Gratia et Libero Arbitrio*, VI.15). Not "their merits are an illusion," but "their merits are His gifts." Merit is real. It is also grace. These are not contradictory propositions. They are the Catholic position.

Augustine also affirmed the sacrificial nature of the Mass, the authority of the Church to bind doctrine, and the necessity of baptismal regeneration — none of which survive in the Reformed system. If Calvin wishes to claim Augustine, he must take all of Augustine. And all of Augustine is Catholic.

## Exchange 3 — Romans and the Law

**CALVIN:**

Let us return to the text of Scripture, where the issue is settled most clearly. In Romans 4, Paul takes up the case of Abraham — the father of all who believe — and demolishes any notion that justification comes through works. "For if Abraham were justified by works, he hath whereof to glory; but not before God. For what saith the scripture? Abraham believed God, and it was counted unto him for righteousness" (Rom. 4:2–3). The verb is *elogisthe* — it was reckoned, credited, counted. This is the language of imputation, not of infusion. Abraham's faith was not itself a righteous work that God rewarded. It was the instrument by which God's promise was received.

Paul presses the point further: "Now to him that worketh is the reward not reckoned of grace, but of debt. But to him that worketh not, but believeth on him that justifieth the ungodly, his faith is counted for righteousness" (Rom. 4:4–5). The contrast could not be sharper. The one who works earns a wage — that is debt, not grace. The one who does not work but believes — this is the one whose faith is credited as righteousness. Paul is not merely excluding circumcision or ceremonial law. He is excluding every form of human working as a ground of justification. The "works of the law" in Paul's argument include the entire law — moral, ceremonial, and civil — because the law in its totality reveals human sinfulness and inability.

Moreover, Paul explicitly notes the chronology: Abraham was justified by faith in Genesis 15, but circumcision was not given until Genesis 17. The sign followed the reality. Circumcision did not contribute to Abraham's justified status; it sealed what was already accomplished by faith. This demolishes the Catholic position that sacraments — baptism being the New Covenant counterpart to circumcision — are instrumental causes of justification. The instrument is faith, and faith alone. The signs attest; they do not effect.

**BELLARMINE:**

Calvin's reading of Romans 4 depends on a false dichotomy that Paul himself does not draw. When Paul contrasts faith and "works of the law," he is writing to specific churches facing a specific controversy: whether Gentile converts must submit to the Mosaic Law — circumcision, dietary regulations, Sabbath observance — in order to be justified. The "works of the law" (*erga nomou*) that Paul excludes are not "any and all human cooperation with grace." They are the distinctive covenant markers of the old dispensation.

This is not a novel Catholic reading; it is the plain context of Galatians. Paul rebukes Peter for withdrawing from table fellowship with Gentiles: "But when I saw that they walked not uprightly according to the truth of the gospel, I said unto Cephas before them all: If thou, being a Jew, livest after the manner of the Gentiles, and not as the Jews, why dost thou compel the Gentiles to live as do the Jews?" (Gal. 2:14, DRB). The controversy is Jewish-Gentile relations and the obsolescence of the Mosaic covenant, not whether charity cooperates with faith in justification.

Paul himself, in the very letter to the Galatians that Calvin marshals for his cause, writes: "For in Christ Jesus neither circumcision availeth any thing, nor uncircumcision: but faith that worketh by charity" (Gal. 5:6, DRB). *Fides quae per caritatem operatur* — faith working through love. Not faith standing alone. Not faith inert and isolated from its fruits. Faith animated by charity. This is precisely the Catholic doctrine of *fides formata* — faith formed by charity — which Trent affirms as the faith that justifies.

As for Abraham: yes, he was justified by faith in Genesis 15. But James cites the same verse — "Abraham believed God, and it was reputed to him to justice" — and then asks: "Was not Abraham our father justified by works, offering up Isaac his son upon the altar? Seest thou that faith did co-work with his works, and by works faith was made perfect?" (James 2:21–22, DRB). Abraham's faith was not a single moment of forensic crediting. It was a living reality that grew, was tested, and was brought to completion through obedience. The Catholic position does not deny that Abraham was justified by faith. It denies that he was justified by faith *alone* — by a faith severed from the love and obedience that are its proper fruit and form.

## Exchange 4 — Assurance and Merit

**CALVIN:**

The pastoral fruit of the Reformed doctrine is the assurance of salvation. Because justification rests entirely upon the finished work of Christ and is received by faith alone, the believer can have a confident and settled assurance that he belongs to God. The Westminster Confession teaches that "such as truly believe in the Lord Jesus, and love him in sincerity, endeavouring to walk in all good conscience before him, may, in this life, be certainly assured that they are in the state of grace" (Westminster Confession, XVIII.1). This is not presumption. It is the normal Christian experience flowing from a gospel of free grace.

The Catholic system destroys this assurance. Trent declared: "No one can know with a certainty of faith, which cannot be subject to error, that he has obtained the grace of God" (Trent, Session VI, Chapter IX). The Catholic must live in perpetual uncertainty — hoping he is in a state of grace but never knowing. He must maintain his justification through the sacraments, avoid mortal sin (which destroys justification entirely), and accumulate merit sufficient to satisfy the justice of God. This is not the Gospel. This is a treadmill. The conscience is never at rest because the ground of salvation is always partly in the believer's own performance.

And this brings us to the deepest error of the Catholic system: the doctrine of merit. Trent teaches that the justified "through the observance of the commandments of God and of the Church, faith co-operating with good works, increase in that justice which they have received through the grace of Christ, and are still further justified" (Trent, Session VI, Chapter X). To speak of man "increasing in justice" before God, of being "further justified" through works — this is precisely what Paul condemns. It makes grace contingent upon human performance. It robs Christ of His glory. As Turretin insists, merit and grace are contradictory categories: "If of grace, it is no more of works; and if of works, it is no more of grace" (Rom. 11:6; Turretin, *Institutes*, XVII.v.4). The Reformed position alone preserves the pure gratuity of the Gospel. The Catholic system, for all its sophistication, has reintroduced the very works-righteousness that Paul spent his apostleship dismantling.

**BELLARMINE:**

Calvin's doctrine of assurance is psychologically appealing, but it is not what Scripture teaches. St. Paul himself writes: "With fear and trembling work out your salvation" (Phil. 2:12, DRB). Fear and trembling are incompatible with the settled certainty Calvin describes. Paul also writes: "I am not conscious to myself of any thing, yet am I not hereby justified; but he that judgeth me is the Lord" (1 Cor. 4:4, DRB). Even the Apostle does not claim infallible certainty of his own state. He writes: "I chastise my body, and bring it into subjection: lest perhaps, when I have preached to others, I myself should become a castaway" (1 Cor. 9:27, DRB). If Paul can fear becoming a castaway, on what grounds does the Calvinist claim absolute certainty?

Trent does not teach despair. It teaches a holy sobriety. We have genuine confidence in God's mercy and the sufficiency of Christ's sacrifice. We do not have the kind of certainty that makes further obedience unnecessary. The Catholic position is that salvation is a real and living relationship with God that can be wounded by grave sin and must be sustained by grace through the sacraments and the life of charity. This is not a treadmill; it is a marriage. A husband who says "I married my wife once and therefore need never again show her love" has not understood marriage. The relationship is real, and real relationships require ongoing fidelity.

Now to merit. Calvin claims that merit and grace are contradictory. Trent says the opposite: "If any one saith, that the good works of one that is justified are in such manner the gifts of God, that they are not also the good merits of him that is justified; or, that the said justified, by the good works which he performs through the grace of God and the merit of Jesus Christ, whose living member he is, does not truly merit increase of grace, eternal life, and the attainment of that eternal life — if so be, however, that he depart in grace — and also an increase of glory: let him be anathema" (Trent, Session VI, Canon XXXII). Note what Trent says: good works are *both* gifts of God *and* true merits. These are not contradictions. When God crowns our merits, He crowns His own gifts — as Augustine taught. The vine gives life to the branches, and the branches bear fruit. The fruit is truly the branch's fruit. It is also, and more fundamentally, the vine's gift. "Without me you can do nothing" (John 15:5, DRB). Catholic merit is not Pelagian bootstrapping. It is the fruit of grace operating through the free cooperation of the human will — a will that has itself been healed and elevated by grace.

The distinction between condign and congruent merit clarifies further. Condign merit — merit in the strict sense, based on a proportion between work and reward — exists only because God has freely promised to reward the works done in grace. It is not that human works are intrinsically worthy of eternal life. It is that God, in His liberality, has established a covenant by which He binds Himself to reward what He Himself produces in us. The merit is real because God's promise is real. But the ground of the promise is grace, not human adequacy.

---

## Closing Statements

**CALVIN:**

I have pressed the case that Scripture alone can bear: that justification is by faith alone, through grace alone, in Christ alone. The righteousness by which we stand before God is not our own — not even our own as renewed by grace — but the perfect righteousness of Christ imputed to us and received by the empty hand of faith. This is the Gospel that freed the conscience of Christendom from the tyranny of uncertainty. I commend the reader to the Scriptures, to Paul above all, and to the God who justifies the ungodly — not by making them first worthy, but by declaring them righteous for the sake of His Son. *Soli Deo Gloria.*

**BELLARMINE:**

The Catholic Church does not oppose faith to works, nor grace to merit, nor Christ's righteousness to the believer's transformation. She holds what Scripture holds — the whole of it, including James, including Paul's "faith working through charity," including our Lord's own words: "Not every one that saith to me, Lord, Lord, shall enter into the kingdom of heaven: but he that doth the will of my Father" (Matt. 7:21, DRB). Justification is by faith — but not by faith alone. It is by a faith that is alive, formed by charity, sustained by the sacraments, and brought to completion through perseverance in grace. God does not merely declare us righteous while leaving us corrupt. He makes us new creatures. He gives us a share in His own divine life. And the works that flow from that life are not an affront to grace — they are its fruit, and God, who is faithful, will not fail to reward what He Himself has planted. *Ad maiorem Dei gloriam.*
