Numbers

Cornelius à Lapide SJ · Biblical Commentary

In Numbers, à Lapide follows Israel through the wilderness, interpreting the journeys, murmurings, and divine ordinances as figures of the soul's pilgrimage toward God and the life of grace.

Chapter 1

Verse 1

God commands Moses to number all the sons of Israel from twenty years old and upward, all who are able to go out to war; the census reflects God's providential ordering of His people into a holy army.

Verse 2

The total of those numbered was 603,550 fighting men, not counting the Levites; the Levites are set apart for service of the Tabernacle and are not enrolled in the military census.

Verse 47

The tribe of Levi is exempted from the census and given charge of the Tabernacle; "among the children of Israel they shall have no possession," for the Lord is their inheritance.

Chapter 2

Verse 2

The twelve tribes are arranged around the Tabernacle in four great companies — Judah to the east, Reuben to the south, Ephraim to the west, Dan to the north — with God in the midst; the whole camp is ordered around divine worship.

Chapter 3

Verse 6

The Levites are applied to Aaron and the priests for service in the Tabernacle; Lapide notes that the Levites are like soldiers who stand guard between God and the people, mediating by their sacred ministry.

Verse 7a

The names of the Levite clans contain moral teaching: Gershon = "stranger" (Ecclesiastics must live as pilgrims on earth); Caath = "sorrowful" (clerics must mourn the sins of the people); Merari = "bitterness" (they must meditate on death and novissima); Levi = "brightness of purity."

Verse 12

God takes the Levites in place of the firstborn of Israel; all firstborn belong to God from the night of the Exodus, and the Levites serve as their substitutes in perpetual sacred ministry.

Chapter 4

Verse 3

The Levites serve from age thirty to fifty; Lapide notes that among the Hebrews no one could teach before age thirty — hence both Christ and John the Baptist began their public preaching at thirty, "the age of mature judgment and rightful authority."

Verse 4

The office of the Kohathites is to carry the most sacred vessels — the Ark, the altar, the candlestick — on their shoulders; no cart is allowed for these, signifying that sacred things must be borne by those specially consecrated.

Chapter 5

Verse 2

The unclean — lepers, those with a flux, those defiled by the dead — are expelled from the camp; God dwells in the midst of the camp and demands ritual purity; not merely external but interior purity of soul.

Verse 7

The guilty party must confess the sin and make restitution, adding a fifth part; this foreshadows the requirement of confession and satisfaction in the Sacrament of Penance.

Verse 12-13

The law of the bitter waters for wives suspected of adultery; God alone can reveal hidden sins; the wife's innocence or guilt is manifested by a miraculous divine judgment.

Verse 28

If the woman is not defiled, she shall be free and shall conceive children; the ordeal vindicates the innocent. God is the supreme judge of secret sins.

Chapter 6

Verse 2a

The Nazirites were devout Jews who separated themselves from common life to dedicate themselves wholly to God — either perpetually (like Samson, Samuel) or for a set time (like St. Paul, Acts 21:23); they abstained from wine and all intoxicants.

Verse 2b

Allegorically, the Nazirites foreshadow the Religious life in the New Law; Lapide cites St. Gregory Nazianzen, St. Thomas (2.2 q.186 a.6), and others: "Nazirites prefigure the life of our Religious" — those separated from the common life of men and consecrated to God by three vows.

Verse 5

The uncut hair of the Nazirite is the sign of his consecration — like a crown upon his head; "let his hair grow, for the consecration of God is upon his head." Tropologically, uncut hair signifies holy thoughts and knowledge adorning the mind.

Verse 5a

Lapide quotes Isidore: the use of the clerical tonsure derives from the example of the Nazirites; Bede adds that the corona tonsure commemorates the crown of thorns of Christ, signifying that clerics bear the sign of His Passion.

Verse 6

The Nazirite must not go near a dead body, not even for father or mother; he exceeds even ordinary priests in this; Religious must cut off all carnal affections for parents and kin, hearing Christ's "Follow me; let the dead bury their dead."

Verse 9

If someone dies before a Nazirite unexpectedly, the days of his vow are nullified and he must begin anew; this signifies that God desires unbroken holiness, and Religious who fall into negligence must begin anew with fresh resolve.

Verse 18

At the completion of the vow the Nazirite shaves his head at the door of the Tabernacle and burns his hair in the fire of the peace-offering; the hair is burned because it was consecrated to God — the whole person is offered to Him.

Verse 21

The vow of Naziriteship is called the "law of his consecration" and a status of perfection in the Old Law; as Lapide explains, it corresponds to Religious life in the New Law, which D. Thomas calls "the state of perfection to be acquired."

Verse 23-24

Aaron and his sons are commanded to bless Israel with the Priestly Blessing: "The Lord bless thee and keep thee"; the triple repetition of the divine name in the three clauses signifies the Holy Trinity, from whom all blessings come.

Verse 25

The Lord show His face to thee and have mercy on thee — Lapide: God's face illumining means God directing all one's acts as a lamp; cited Ps 4:7 "The light of thy face is signed upon us, O Lord."

Verse 26

The Lord turn His countenance toward thee and give thee peace; Lapide notes the efficacy of the sacerdotal blessing even in the Old Law: God promises to hear and fulfill it (v.27), as He fulfilled the blessings of dying fathers.

Verse 27

They shall invoke my name upon the children of Israel, and I will bless them; the priestly blessing in both Testaments is efficacious; Catholics rightly seek and receive it devoutly.

Chapter 7

Verse 1

On the day Moses completed and anointed the Tabernacle, the princes of the twelve tribes brought offerings — six wagons and twelve oxen — and over twelve days each prince offered his individual gifts for the dedication of the altar.

Verse 89

When Moses entered the Tabernacle to speak with God, he heard the divine voice speaking to him from above the mercy-seat between the two Cherubim; God condescends to speak with Moses face to face "as a man speaks with his friend."

Chapter 8

Verse 2

The seven lamps of the golden candlestick are set so they illuminate the table of showbread; the seven lamps signify the sevenfold gifts of the Holy Spirit illumining the bread of God's word.

Verse 24-26

Levites serve from age 25 to 50; after 50 they assist their brethren but no longer bear the heavy burdens; a principle of graduated service and honourable retirement for those grown old in sacred ministry.

Chapter 9

Verse 1-5

God commands the celebration of Passover in the desert of Sinai in the second year; this shows that the Passover is not a one-time event but a perpetual feast — it was to be kept in Canaan for all generations.

Verse 10-11

Those defiled by a corpse or away on a journey may observe the Passover on the fourteenth of the second month; God provides for those unable to fulfil a sacred duty through no fault of their own.

Verse 15-16

The cloud of the Lord covered the Tabernacle by day and the appearance of fire by night; the cloud and fire are signs of God's continual presence guiding Israel — "continually," day and night.

Verse 18-23

Israel moved or remained encamped entirely at the Lord's command, signified by the lifting or resting of the cloud; perfect obedience to divine providence — "by the word of the Lord they pitched their tents, and by the word of the Lord they marched."

Chapter 10

Verse 2

Two silver trumpets are made for assembling the people and moving the camp; silver signifies the brightness of preaching, which calls souls to penance and to battle for God.

Verse 9

When Israel goes out to war, the trumpets sound before the Lord; "and you shall be remembered before the Lord your God, and shall be saved from your enemies." Prayer and liturgical invocation precede every holy warfare.

Verse 33-35

The Ark of the covenant preceded the camp by three days' journey to seek out a resting place; Moses prays: "Arise, O Lord, and let thine enemies be scattered." The Ark typifies Christ going before His people to prepare their way.

Chapter 11

Verse 1a

The people murmur as if groaning under labour; God's anger is kindled and fire consumes the extremity of the camp (Taberah = Conflagration); Lapide identifies the cause as concupiscence for the flesh-pots of Egypt, not mere fatigue.

Verse 4-5

The mixed multitude craved flesh; they wept remembering the fish, cucumbers, melons, leeks, onions, and garlic of Egypt: "Our soul is dry and our eyes see nothing but this manna." The concupiscence of the flesh loathes heavenly food.

Verse 5a

Moral commentary on the nature of the mob: Lapide assembles fourteen maxims from Philo, Plutarch, Tacitus, Polybius, Antisthenes, and others, concluding: the multitude is governed by passion, not reason; it craves what it was accustomed to, and abhors what is new and heavenly.

Verse 7

The manna was like coriander seed, the colour of bdellium; yet despite its miraculous variety of taste (Wis 16:20), the people were nauseated because the form, colour, and manner remained constant; the concupiscent eye craves novelty above all.

Verse 11-15

Moses cries to the Lord: "Why hast thou laid the burden of all this people upon me?... I am not able to bear all this people alone, for it is too heavy for me." Even the greatest saints are burdened by the weight of pastoral care.

Verse 16-17

God commands Moses to gather 70 elders; "I will take of thy spirit and will give to them, that they may bear the burden of the people with thee." True authority is shared, not diminished; the spirit given to others is not lost to Moses.

Verse 25

The Spirit rested on the 70 elders and they prophesied; Eldad and Medad also prophesied in the camp — prophetic gifts are poured out freely, not confined to official channels.

Verse 29

Moses' magnanimous response to Joshua's complaint: "Why art thou jealous for me? Would that all the people were prophets and the Lord gave them his spirit!" A model of holy unselfishness in spiritual authority.

Verse 31-34

God sends quails from the sea; while the flesh is still between their teeth, the Lord's anger blazes and He strikes the people with a very great plague; the place is called Kibroth-hattaavah (Sepulchres of Concupiscence). Concupiscence punished by its own satisfaction.

Chapter 12

Verse 1

Miriam and Aaron speak against Moses on account of his Ethiopian wife; allegorically, Moses' marriage to an Ethiopian woman prefigures the marriage of Christ with the Church of the Gentiles (St. Ambrose, St. Bernard).

Verse 3

Moses was a very meek man, above all men upon earth; because of his meekness he did not answer his detractors, and God Himself took up his defence. Meekness merits divine protection and familiar intercourse with God.

Verse 10

Miriam is struck with leprosy white as snow; she alone is punished (not Aaron) because she instigated the rebellion; Aaron as high priest could not bear the disgrace of leprosy without dishonour to the sacred ministry.

Verse 10a

Allegory (St. Ambrose): Miriam's leprosy typifies the Synagogue's infection of unbelief, murmuring against the marriage of Moses (Christ) with the Ethiopian woman (the Gentile Church); at the end the Synagogue will be healed by faith.

Verse 13

Moses prays for his sister who slandered him: "Heal her, O God, I beseech thee"; an example of intercession for enemies surpassed only by St. Stephen, who prayed for Saul without being asked.

Verse 14-15

Miriam is excluded from the camp seven days; the whole people waited for her return and did not continue the march — even the sinner's exclusion is a corrective mercy.

Chapter 13

Verse 2-3

Moses sends twelve spies, one from each tribe, all princes; the choice of explorers is itself a military art, for "the best general is he who has most thoroughly learned the affairs of his enemies" (Cabrias, cited by Lapide).

Verse 17

Moses renames Hoshea son of Nun as Joshua (Jehoshua = "the Lord saves"), adding the first letter of the Tetragrammaton; this promises salvation to him and through him to all Israel, and foreshadows the name "Jesus."

Verse 27-28

The spies report: the land flows with milk and honey — here are its fruits! — but the inhabitants are powerful, the cities great and walled; the children of Anak are there. Truth and courage require acknowledging both good and difficulty.

Verse 31-33

Ten spies spread an evil report: "The land devours its inhabitants; we seemed like grasshoppers before the Nephilim." Pusillanimity of unbelief: comparing themselves to insects while forgetting the almighty God who was with them.

Chapter 14

Verse 1-4

The whole congregation wept, murmured against Moses and Aaron, and proposed returning to Egypt under a new captain; the folly of the rebels: returning to Egypt through hostile desert was practically impossible.

Verse 7-9

Caleb and Joshua plead: "The land is very good; if the Lord is favourable, He will bring us in and give it to us. Do not rebel against the Lord; fear not the people of the land, for they are bread for us."

Verse 12

God threatens to destroy Israel and make Moses "a great nation mightier than this one"; the intercession of Moses follows as the model of priestly mediation.

Verse 17-19

Moses' intercession: "Let the power of the Lord be magnified... The Lord is patient and of great mercy, forgiving iniquity and sin... forgive the iniquity of this people." The prayer of Moses is a model of priestly intercession for sinful people.

Verse 20

I have forgiven, according to thy word — the efficacy of priestly intercession; God's pardon is given in response to Moses' prayer, establishing the pattern of penitential mediation.

Verse 22-23

Those who tested God ten times and did not obey His voice shall not see the Promised Land; all men from 20 years and above die in the desert. The number ten symbolises complete obstinacy in unbelief.

Verse 24

My servant Caleb, who has followed me with a full heart (spiritu alio), I will bring into the land he scouted. Wholehearted faith merits the reward; Caleb alone among the ten is singled out for his courage.

Verse 34

Forty years of desert wandering, one year for each day of the scouting — "you shall know my vengeance." The gravity of unbelief and murmuring against God is measured by its consequences upon an entire generation.

Chapter 15

Verse 30

The soul that does anything presumptuously, whether he is native or stranger, the same blasphemes the Lord; that soul shall be cut off from among his people. Presumptuous sin is graver than ignorance; it scorns the known law of God.

Verse 38-40

God commands fringes on the corners of garments; they are a perpetual reminder of all the commandments of the Lord. The external sign serves the interior recollection of duty.

Chapter 16

Verse 1-3

Core, Dathan, Abiron, and On with 250 princes rebel against Moses and Aaron: "All the congregation is holy and the Lord is among them; why do you exalt yourselves above the people of the Lord?" A type of all clerical and lay ambition against legitimate hierarchical authority.

Verse 5

Moses answers by offering a test at the Tabernacle: God will show who is His; "He who is holy, Him shall He bring near to Himself." Let God judge the presumptuous; do not pre-empt divine election.

Verse 31-33

The earth opens and swallows Core, Dathan, and Abiron alive with their households; all Israel who stood around fled at their cry: "Lest the earth swallow us also." An unparalleled punishment for sacrilegious usurpation of the priesthood.

Verse 35

Fire goes out from the Lord and consumes the 250 men who offered incense; their bronze censers are beaten flat and attached to the altar as a permanent warning against unauthorized liturgical ministry.

Verse 46-48

Aaron takes fire from the altar, puts incense on it, and runs into the midst of the congregation to make atonement; "he stood between the dead and the living, and the plague was stopped." Aaron as intercessor between God's wrath and the dying people is a type of Christ.

Chapter 17

Verse 2

God commands Moses to collect twelve staffs from the twelve tribal princes; each man writes his name on his own staff; Aaron's name is written on the staff of Levi. The dispute over the priesthood is submitted to divine arbitration.

Verse 8a

Aaron's staff sprouted, burst into blossom, and bore ripe almonds overnight — a triple miracle: bud, flower, and ripe fruit simultaneously; all the other staffs remained dead. A manifest sign of divine election.

Verse 8b

St. Augustine (Serm. 3 de Temp.): "The rod of Aaron was the Virgin Mary, who conceived and bore for us Christ the priest of all things. That the rod produced nuts is an image of the Lord's body, which has a triple substance: skin, shell, and kernel — in the skin the Saviour's flesh, in the shell His bones, in the kernel His interior divine nature."

Verse 8c

Allegorically (Rupert, St. Bernard, Hom. 2 super Missus): the rod is the Virgin Mary; the flower is Christ, according to Isaiah 11:1: "A rod shall come forth from the root of Jesse, and a flower shall rise from his root." Aaron's rod signifies the virginal Mother from whom the eternal Priest bloomed without male seed.

Verse 8d

The almond tree (amygdalus) is the first to flower among trees and the first to produce fruit; its name in Hebrew means "wakeful" (shaqed); thus Aaron's almond rod signifies priestly vigilance, and also the Resurrection — Christ "the firstfruits of those who sleep" rose first of all.

Verse 10

Aaron's rod is preserved before the Ark as a perpetual memorial and sign against rebels; Lapide notes it was stored with the manna and the tablets of the Law — three testimonies of God's special mercy to Israel.

Verse 12-13

Israel cries: "We are consumed, we are all undone; everyone who comes near the Tabernacle dies!" Their terror at God's holiness leads to institution of the priestly mediation described in chapter 18.

Chapter 18

Verse 1

Aaron and his sons bear the iniquity of the sanctuary; if any layman comes too near and dies, the priests are held responsible — they stand between God's holiness and the people's weakness.

Verse 8

God grants to Aaron the custody of the firstfruits and holy offerings; all that is sanctified belongs to the priests as their perpetual due — God Himself is their inheritance, not any earthly portion.

Verse 21

God assigns tithes to the Levites as their inheritance in return for their service; from these tithes the Levites in turn give a tithe to the priests — the layered system of sacred support for those who serve the altar.

Chapter 19

Verse 2

The red heifer without blemish, on which no yoke has come; the red colour symbolises sin — "sins are as scarlet" — for this heifer was offered as a sin-offering on behalf of the whole people.

Verse 9

The ashes of the heifer mixed with running water make the water of purification (aqua lustrationis); those defiled by contact with the dead are purified; contact with a corpse signifies contact with mortal sin, the death of the soul.

Verse 11-12

Whoever touches a dead body is unclean seven days; he must be sprinkled on the third and seventh day; otherwise he shall not be purified. Type of the sinner's need for sacramental cleansing after the death of grace.

Chapter 20

Verse 1

The people come to the desert of Sin in the first month of the fortieth year; Miriam dies and is buried at Kadesh; the well of water which God gave Israel through Miriam's merits departs at her death (according to rabbinic tradition noted by Lapide).

Verse 7-8

God commands Moses to speak to the rock; Moses instead strikes it twice in anger: "Hear, ye rebels! Can we fetch water for you out of this rock?" Moses' impatience is a small sin but sufficient to exclude him from Canaan.

Verse 12

God's rebuke of Moses and Aaron: "Because you did not believe in me to sanctify me before the children of Israel, therefore you shall not bring this assembly into the land." Even small failures of faith in leaders carry grave consequences.

Verse 25-28

On Mount Hor, Moses strips Aaron of his high-priestly vestments and puts them on Eleazar his son; Aaron dies on the mountain top. The solemn transfer of the high-priestly office: vestments pass to the heir.

Chapter 21

Verse 5

The people murmur again: "Why have you brought us out of Egypt to die in the wilderness? There is no bread and no water, and our soul loathes this very light food (manna)." New murmuring, new punishment — the eighth general murmuring of Israel in the desert.

Verse 6

God sends fiery serpents (seraphim); their bite causes such heat that the bitten feel as if burning. The punishment fits the crime: murmurers "sharpen their tongues like a serpent" (Ps 57), and so a serpent is made their executioner.

Verse 8-9

God commands Moses to make a bronze serpent and raise it on a standard; whoever looked at it was healed — not by any natural virtue in the bronze, but by a miraculous divine act. The bronze serpent was "a sign and moral instrument of this cure."

Verse 9a

Allegorical/typological: the serpent of bronze raised on wood signifies Christ raised on the Cross in the form of a condemned sinner but without sin; by gazing upon Him with faith and contrition we are healed of the deadly bites of sin.

Verse 9b

Christ Himself confirms this: "As Moses lifted up the serpent in the desert, so must the Son of Man be lifted up, that all who believe in Him may not perish but have life everlasting" (John 3:14). Confirmed by Tertullian, Augustine, Theodoret, Gregory of Nyssa.

Verse 9c

St. Augustine: the serpent of brass signifies also the divinity of Christ — brass is incorruptible and quasi-eternal — and the resonant glory of the Cross that has spread to all nations. The bronze serpent is taraph (fiery), signifying Christ's burning love on the Cross (John 3:16).

Verse 16-18

God shows the thirsty people a well; the princes dig it with their staves and Israel sings: "Spring up, O well! Sing ye to it." A canticle of joy for divine providence; the well signifies the Law and wisdom given through Moses, the "giver of the law."

Chapter 22

Verse 5

Balak sends messengers to Balaam: "Come, curse this people for me; for they are mightier than I." Balaam was a true diviner — an ariolus who sought auguries from extispicy; he is an example of the prophet who has the gift of vision but corrupt will.

Verse 22-35

The angel of the Lord bars Balaam's way; the ass sees the angel and refuses to advance; Balaam strikes the ass and God opens its mouth to rebuke him. Even God's rebuke is mediated through a dumb creature to shame the prophet.

Chapter 23

Verse 8

How shall I curse whom God has not cursed? How shall I denounce whom the Lord has not denounced? Balaam cannot curse those whom God blesses; a consolation for those persecuted by enemies: the blessing of God cannot be nullified by human curses.

Verse 10

Let my soul die the death of the just, and my last end be like to theirs! Even the wicked Balaam, illuminated by grace, desires the death of the righteous; but he does not desire their life — a deadly contradiction, noted by St. Gregory.

Verse 19

God is not a man that He should lie, nor as the son of man that He should change. Hath He then said, and will He not do it? God's immutability grounds the certainty of His promises; four reasons why men break their word do not apply to God.

Verse 21

There is no idol in Jacob, nor is any divining seen in Israel; the Lord his God is with him, and the sound of the king's triumph is in him. Israel praised for pure worship of God and the absence of divination — contrasted with the nations.

Verse 22

God brought him out of Egypt; his strength is like a unicorn (rhinoceros). Lapide's extended excursus on the rhinoceros and monoceros — the rhinoceros symbolises God's slow but terrible power; the monoceros (unicorn) tamed by a virgin = Christ captured only in the womb of the Virgin.

Verse 23

There is no augury in Jacob, nor divination in Israel; in due time it shall be told to Jacob and Israel what God has wrought. Israel's prophetic gifts — given through Moses, the priestly ephod, and the Prophets — replace the false augury of the nations.

Chapter 24

Verse 5

How beautiful are thy tabernacles, O Jacob, and thy tents, O Israel! The tents of Israel are compared to shady valleys, to gardens by rivers, to tabernacles fixed by the Lord, to cedars beside the waters — fourfold comparison signifying beauty, fruitfulness, divine stability, and exaltation.

Verse 7

Water shall flow from his bucket, and his seed shall be in many waters. Lapide: Israel's posterity will be fecund; the Chaldaean paraphrast reads it of the kings and ultimately the Messiah springing from Israel's seed.

Verse 17a

I shall see him, but not now; I shall behold him, but not nigh. A star shall rise out of Jacob, and a sceptre shall spring up from Israel. The great Messianic prophecy of Balaam.

Verse 17b

The Chaldaean Targum translates explicitly: "A king shall rise from the house of Jacob, and the Christ shall be anointed from the house of Israel, and shall slay the princes of Moab, and shall rule over all the children of men."

Verse 17c

Christ is called a "star" because He is the Light of the world; He is the "morning star" (Apoc 22:16) — He dispels the darkness of unbelief and mortality; He announced the dawn of eternal day. Lapide traces Christ's six virtues to the six rays of a star.

Verse 17d

The Magi were descendants or successors of Balaam; knowing this prophecy from their ancestor, when they saw the star at Bethlehem they immediately recognised it as the star of the promised King. The Magi said: "We have seen his star" — a star known, not unknown (Origen, Cyprian, Ambrose, Augustine, Chrysostom).

Verse 24

Ships shall come from Italy and shall overcome the Assyrians, and shall afflict the Hebrews, and at the last they also shall perish. Lapide takes this as a prophecy of Rome — the Romans who subdued both the Near Eastern powers and eventually Jerusalem — and of Rome's own eventual end.

Chapter 25

Verse 1-3

Israel fornicates with the daughters of Moab and worships Baal-Peor; this was Balaam's counsel to Balak — if you cannot curse them by prophecy, corrupt them through women. "Beauty conquers the armed; charm captures the sword" (Origen's Balaam).

Verse 7-8

Phinehas seizes a spear and runs through Zimri and Cozbi; the plague ceases after 24,000 have died. Holy zeal for God's honour expressed in decisive action saves the rest of Israel.

Verse 11-13

God rewards Phinehas with the "covenant of peace" and "an everlasting covenant of priesthood for him and his seed after him, because he was zealous for his God and made atonement for the children of Israel." Priestly zeal expiates communal sin.

Chapter 26

Verse 2

A second census is taken after the plague; the total is 601,730 — slightly less than the first. God's justice reduces the faithless generation; God's mercy preserves the remnant.

Chapter 27

Verse 1-4

The daughters of Zelophehad petition for their father's inheritance; Lapide praises their just and prudent appeal to Moses, Eleazar, and the princes at the Tabernacle door — a model of lawful recourse to legitimate authority.

Verse 6-7

God approves their petition: "The daughters of Zelophehad speak what is just; give them a possession among the brethren of their father and transfer their father's inheritance to them." God's law is not limited by human legal custom when justice demands.

Verse 16-17

Moses prays for a successor: "Let the Lord God of the spirits of all flesh provide a man who may be over this multitude... that the congregation be not as sheep without a shepherd." The pastoral concern of the dying leader.

Verse 18-20

God designates Joshua, "a man in whom the Spirit is," to succeed Moses; Moses lays his hands on Joshua before all the congregation — the laying on of hands as the rite of conferring authority and the Spirit.

Chapter 28

Verse 2

Command the children of Israel and say to them: My oblation, my bread for my burnt-offerings of most sweet savour, you shall offer to me in their season. The daily perpetual sacrifice (tamid) must never be omitted — the pattern of the liturgical hours.

Verse 9-10

A double burnt-offering on the Sabbath, over and above the daily offering; the Sabbath liturgy is distinguished by additional sacrifices — greater holiness requires greater external worship.

Chapter 29

Verse 1-2

The Feast of Trumpets (Rosh Hashanah) with solemn sacrifices; Nm 29:7-8 the Day of Atonement (Yom Kippur) with affliction of souls; Nm 29:12 the Feast of Tabernacles with fourteen days of sacrifice — the liturgical year ordered for atonement and joy.

Chapter 30

Verse 2

When a man shall make a vow to the Lord, or bind himself by an oath, he shall not break his word but shall do all that he promised. The sacredness of vows; Lapide compares this to religious vows in the New Law, which are binding under pain of grave sin.

Verse 3-8

A woman's vow may be annulled by her father or husband on the day he hears it; if he is silent, the vow stands. Lapide: husbands and fathers have authority to direct the devotional life of their households, provided they do so for good reason.

Chapter 31

Verse 2

Vengeance on the Midianites for their seduction of Israel at Baal-Peor; the military campaign is commanded by God before the death of Moses — holy war in defence of divine honour.

Verse 8

Balaam is slain in the battle; he who corrupted Israel by his counsel dies with those he had tried to serve. "The wages of sin is death" — even for the prophet who spoke true oracles.

Chapter 32

Verse 6-13

Moses rebukes Reuben and Gad for asking to settle east of the Jordan: "Shall your brothers go to war while you sit here? Why do you discourage the heart of the children of Israel?" The duty of solidarity — none may exempt themselves from the common struggle.

Chapter 33

Verse 1-4

Enumeration of the 42 stations of the Exodus journey; Lapide notes that Origen and St. Jerome allegorised these as the 42 stages of the soul's spiritual journey from Egypt (the world) to the Promised Land (heaven).

Chapter 35

Verse 6

Six cities of refuge for involuntary manslayers, three on each side of the Jordan, among the 48 Levitical cities; the Levites as sacred mediators administer the right of asylum.

Verse 11-12

The city of refuge protects the involuntary manslayer until his case is judged by the congregation; the blood-avenger may not touch him within its walls. The rule of law and due process even for the guilty.

Verse 25

The manslayer must remain in the city of refuge until the death of the high priest who was anointed with holy oil; after that he may return freely to his own land.

Verse 25a

Allegorically (Cyril of Alexandria, Lib. 8 de Adorat. 165; St. Gregory, Hom. 6 in Ezek.): the human race was guilty of homicide through sin; penitents fled to the cities of refuge (i.e. the limbo of the Fathers) until the death of the High Priest (Christ), whose death freed them, broke their bonds, and led them out of the underworld.

Verse 33

Defile not the land you inhabit, which is stained with blood; the land cannot be expiated of blood shed therein, except by the blood of him who shed it. The gravity of bloodguilt and the lex talionis as a moral foundation.

Chapter 36

Verse 6-7

The daughters of Zelophehad must marry within their own tribe; this preserves the inheritance of each tribe intact — "the inheritance of the children of Israel shall not be transferred from tribe to tribe."

Verse 13

Final verse: "These are the commandments and judgements which the Lord commanded through Moses to the children of Israel in the plains of Moab upon the Jordan over against Jericho." Conclusion of the Book of Numbers on the edge of the Promised Land.
